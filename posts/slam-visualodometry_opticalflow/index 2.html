Getting into direct method

Feature-based methods in general are good, but:

  • it is pretty slow to calculate descriptor and finding matching (20ms for ORB, 217ms for SURF, 5228ms for SIFT, this is sad for 30Hz robotic system).
  • For an image of 300000 pixels, we probably only use 500 features, aka 0.16% of total pixels. This is a waste of information.
  • or sometimes we go into a wall of no features

Therefore current research trying to replace descriptor+feature matching using direct method.

Feature-Based Method Direct Method
Minimizes Reprojection Error Minimizes Photometric Error
Goal: Make the distance between the “predicted pixel” and “observed pixel” zero. Goal: Make the difference in brightness (grayscale value) zero.
1. Calculate descriptors first to match points then 2. minimize the error. (2 steps) Minimize the error directly (from pixel intensity) (1 step)

Optical Flow

The idea is: an object at time \(t\) at \((x, y)\) is still the same object at time \(t+1\) at \((x+\delta x, y+\delta y)\). We represent the image as a function \(I(x, y, t)\), where the output is the brightness (grayscale value). Our core assumption is: \[I(x + dx, y + dy, t + dt) = I(x, y, t)\]

First order Taylor sweep: \[I(x + dx, y + dy, t + dt) \approx I(x, y, t) + \frac{\partial I}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial I}{\partial t}dt\]

Combining these two equations, we get: \[\frac{\partial I}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial I}{\partial t}dt = 0\] \[\frac{\partial I}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}dy = -\frac{\partial I}{\partial t}dt\] \[\frac{\partial I}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{\partial I}{\partial t}\] \[\frac{\partial I}{\partial x}u + \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}v = -\frac{\partial I}{\partial t}\]

We define our variables to make them easier to work with:

  • \(u = \frac{dx}{dt}, v = \frac{dy}{dt}\): The velocity of the pixel (what we want to find).
  • \(I_x = \frac{\partial I}{\partial x}, I_y = \frac{\partial I}{\partial y}\): The image gradient (how brightness changes as you move across the pixels).
  • \(I_t = \frac{\partial I}{\partial t}\): The temporal gradient (how brightness at a fixed spot changes over time).

\[[I_x \quad I_y] \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = -I_t\]

A little explaination: \[\underbrace{I_x}_{\text{"Local Steepness" (Inside ONE frame)}} \times \underbrace{u}_{\text{"Displacement" (Between TWO frames)}} = \underbrace{-I_t}_{\text{"Brightness Change" (Between TWO frames)}}\]

For example you are hiking and need to find how far you walked, but you only have a barometer (which measures altitude). You use the slope to calculate the distance you walked (diagonal) \[\underbrace{I_x}_{\text{Slope (known)}} \cdot \underbrace{u}_{\text{Distance Walked (to find)}} = \underbrace{-I_t}_{\text{Change in Altitude (can be measured by barometer)}}\]

What we want is to calculate the motion \(u, v\) of the pixel, but this formula has two variables, and we cannot find \(u, v\) by a single pixel. Therefore, additional constraints are needed to calculate \(u, v\). In LK optical flow, we also assume the pixels in a certain window have the same motion. For example with 3x3 window we can find 9 equations.

For a window of size \(w \times w\), we have \(w^2\) pixels. We assume every pixel in that small patch has the exact same velocity \(\begin{bmatrix} u & v \end{bmatrix}^T\). This gives us a set of \(w^2\) equations:

\[[ \mathbf{I}_x \quad \mathbf{I}_y ]_k \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = -\mathbf{I}_{t_k}, \quad k = 1, \dots, w^2\]

we stack the individual gradients into a large matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) and the temporal changes into a vector \(\mathbf{b}\): \[\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} [\mathbf{I}_x, \mathbf{I}_y]_1 \\ \vdots \\ [\mathbf{I}_x, \mathbf{I}_y]_k \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_{t1} \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{I}_{tk} \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\mathbf{A} \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = -\mathbf{b}\]

Because we have more equations (\(w^2\)) than unknowns (\(2\)), the system is overdetermined. There likely isn’t a single \((u, v)\) that is 100% perfect for every pixel due to noise. We find the “best fit” using the Least-Squares method (the Normal Equation):

\[\begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix}^* = -(\mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{A})^{-1} \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b}\]

Direct Method

In the Feature-Based method, we minimized the distance between points (geometry). Here, we minimize the difference in brightness (photometry).

We assume the first camera is at the origin. A 3D point \(\mathbf{P}\) in the world projects to pixel \(\mathbf{p}_1\) in the first image and \(\mathbf{p}_2\) in the second image.In the first image (\(I_1\)):

\[\mathbf{p}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}_1 = \frac{1}{Z_1} \mathbf{K P}\]

In the second image (\(I_2\)):The camera has moved by a transformation \(\mathbf{T}\) (rotation \(\mathbf{R}\) and translation \(\mathbf{t}\)). The point \(\mathbf{P}\) is transformed into the second camera’s coordinate system before being projected:

\[\mathbf{p}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}_2 = \frac{1}{Z_2} \mathbf{K}(\mathbf{R P} + \mathbf{t}) = \frac{1}{Z_2} \mathbf{K}(\mathbf{T P})_{1:3}\]

In Feature-Based SLAM, we match \(\mathbf{p}_1\) and \(\mathbf{p}_2\) using descriptors. In the Direct Method, we don’t know where \(\mathbf{p}_2\) is yet! We only have a “guess” for the camera pose \(\mathbf{T}\).

We define the Photometric Error (\(e\)) as the difference in brightness between the two pixels that (we hope) represent the same 3D point:

\[e = I_1(\mathbf{p}_1) - I_2(\mathbf{p}_2)\]

  • \(I_1(\mathbf{p}_1)\) is the grayscale value at \(\mathbf{p}_1\) in the first image.
  • \(I_2(\mathbf{p}_2)\) is the grayscale value at the projected location \(\mathbf{p}_2\) in the second image.

Our goal is to find the transformation \(\mathbf{T}\) that makes the brightness difference as small as possible across all \(N\) visible points - maybe even all pixels:

\[\min_{\mathbf{T}} J(\mathbf{T}) = \sum_{i=1}^N e_i^2 = \sum_{i=1}^N \|I_1(\mathbf{p}_{1,i}) - I_2(\mathbf{p}_{2,i})\|^2\]

This is still a Non-linear Least Squares problem, just like PnP or ICP, but the “error” is now a brightness difference instead of a coordinate distance.

To solve this with Gauss-Newton, we need to know how the error \(e\) changes when we wiggle the pose \(\mathbf{T}\). We define two intermediate variables to make the Chain Rule easier to manage:

  • \(\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{T P}\): The 3D coordinates of the point in the second camera frame. dependent to \(\mathbf{T}\)
  • \(\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{Z_2} \mathbf{K q}\): The 2D pixel coordinates in the second image (this is just \(\mathbf{p}_2\) without the subscript). dependent to \(\mathbf{q}\), which dependent to \(\mathbf{T}\)

The photometric error is defined as \(e(\mathbf{T}) = I_1(\mathbf{p}_1) - I_2(\mathbf{u})\). Since \(I_1\) is constant, we only differentiate the second term. By the chain rule, the derivative is split into three parts:

\[\frac{\partial e}{\partial \mathbf{T}} = - \underbrace{\frac{\partial I_2}{\partial \mathbf{u}}}_{\text{Term 1}} \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{q}}}_{\text{Term 2}} \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathbf{q}}{\partial \delta \xi}}_{\text{Term 3}}\]

  • Term 1: Image Gradient (\(\frac{\partial I_2}{\partial \mathbf{u}}\)) (new in direct method)

    This is the spatial gradient of the second image at pixel coordinates \(\mathbf{u}\). It is a \(1 \times 2\) vector: \([I_x, I_y]\). It represents the “slope” of the brightness at that specific pixel.

  • Term 2: Projection Derivative (\(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{q}}\))

    This \(2 \times 3\) matrix describes how the pixel coordinates change if the 3D point \(\mathbf{q}\) moves slightly within the camera’s field of view:\[\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{q}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{f_x}{Z} & 0 & -\frac{f_x X}{Z^2} \\ 0 & \frac{f_y}{Z} & -\frac{f_y Y}{Z^2} \end{bmatrix}\]

  • Term 3: Pose Perturbation (\(\frac{\partial \mathbf{q}}{\partial \delta \xi}\))

    This \(3 \times 6\) matrix is our old friend from Lie Algebra. It describes how the 3D point \(\mathbf{q}\) moves relative to the camera when the camera itself wiggles:\[\frac{\partial \mathbf{q}}{\partial \delta \xi} = [\mathbf{I}, -\mathbf{q}^\wedge]\]

In practice, we multiply Term 2 and Term 3 together first. This gives us a \(2 \times 6\) matrix that represents the Geometric Jacobian—how camera motion affects pixel coordinates:\[\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \delta \xi} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{f_x}{Z} & 0 & -\frac{f_x X}{Z^2} & -\frac{f_x XY}{Z^2} & f_x + \frac{f_x X^2}{Z^2} & -\frac{f_x Y}{Z} \\ 0 & \frac{f_y}{Z} & -\frac{f_y Y}{Z^2} & -f_y - \frac{f_y Y^2}{Z^2} & \frac{f_y XY}{Z^2} & \frac{f_y X}{Z} \end{bmatrix}\]

Finally, we multiply by the image gradient to get the Photometric Jacobian for a single point:\[\mathbf{J} = - \frac{\partial I_2}{\partial \mathbf{u}} \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \delta \xi}\]

  • Size: \((1 \times 2) \times (2 \times 6) = \mathbf{1 \times 6}\).
  • Result: A single row of 6 numbers that tells the optimizer exactly how to rotate and translate the camera to make the colors match.

Sum up this:

  • The camera takes a tiny step (\(\delta \xi\)).
  • That step causes the 3D point to move relative to the camera (Term 3).
  • That 3D movement causes the point’s projection to “slide” on the image sensor (Term 2).
  • That “slide” causes the pixel at that spot to change its brightness based on the local texture (Term 1) this is the new thing in this method.

Discussion: for this to work, we need to know the depth (\(Z\)) of the points. to reconstruct \(P\)

  • RGB-D cameras: Depth is given for every pixel.
  • Binocular (Stereo): Depth is calculated via parallax.
  • Monocular: This is the “Hard Mode” of SLAM. You have to estimate the depth over multiple frames as the camera moves, which we will see in another blog. (Structure from Motion or Depth Filter)

FAQ

  • So we still have to find keypoint first? Yes, once again, we first calculate keypoints, then instead of descriptor+feature matching, we directly apply direct method THEREFORE the name!!!

  • BUT… How do we know which \(p_2\) corresponds to \(p_1\)? In feature-based SLAM, we look for \(p_2\) by searching the whole image for a similar descriptor.In the Direct Method, we don’t “find” \(p_2\); we “calculate” it.

    1. We take \(p_1\) and its depth \(Z_1\).
    2. We “unproject” it into 3D space to find its world position \(\mathbf{P}\).
    3. We take our current best guess of the camera pose \(\mathbf{T}\).
    4. We project \(\mathbf{P}\) into the second camera frame using that guess. Wherever it lands is \(p_2\).

  • Is Direct Method just Optical Flow added with camera pose? Yes, in Optical Flow (2D Optimization):

    • We are looking for the pixel velocity \(\mathbf{w} = [u, v]^T\).
    • Error: \(e = I_1(x,y) - I_2(x+u, y+v)\)
    • Jacobian: \(\mathbf{J} = [I_x, I_y]\) (Just the image gradients).
    • Update: We move the pixel in 2D.

    Direct Method (6D Optimization):

    • We are looking for the camera pose \(\xi \in \mathfrak{se}(3)\).
    • Error: \(e = I_1(p_1) - I_2(\text{project}(\mathbf{T} \cdot \text{unproject}(p_1)))\)
    • Jacobian: \(\mathbf{J} = \underbrace{-[I_x, I_y]}_{\text{Optical Flow part}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \delta \xi}}_{\text{The extra "Pose" part}}\)
