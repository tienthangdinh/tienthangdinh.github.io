Determining the Distribution of \(Y\) (basically just Inverse Transform)

When we have a random variable \(X\) and a function \(g(x)\), Y is also a random variable:

\[Y = g(X)\]

The probability of \(Y\) is equal to the probability of the set of all \(X\) values that map into \(B\):

\[P(Y \in B) = P(X \in g^{-1}(B))\]

But how to find this distribution of \(Y\)?

To find the distribution of \(Y\), we HAVE TO START with the CDF - aka the Probability and then differentiate to find the PDF.

  1. Find the Inverse Transform: \[Y = g(X) \iff X = g^{-1}(Y)\]

  2. The CDF of \(Y\) (important inverse transform): \[F_Y(y) = P(Y \le y) = P(g(X) \le y) = P(X \in \{x \mid g(x) \le y\}) = P(X \in g^{-1}(y))\]

  3. The PDF of \(Y\): \[f_Y(y) = \frac{d}{dy}F_Y(y)\]

Example 1: Square Transformation (\(Y = X^2\))

0. Find the Inverse Transform:

\[Y = X^2 \iff X = \pm \sqrt{y}\]

1. Finding the CDF

\[F_Y(y) = P(X^2 \le y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \le X \le \sqrt{y}) = F_X(\sqrt{y}) - F_X(-\sqrt{y})\]

2. Finding the PDF

Derivative with respect to \(y\) using the Chain Rule:

\[f_Y(y) = \frac{d}{dy} [F_X(\sqrt{y}) - F_X(-\sqrt{y})]\] \[f_Y(y) = f_X(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} + f_X(-\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}\] \[f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} [f_X(\sqrt{y}) + f_X(-\sqrt{y})], \quad y > 0\]

3. Apply Number

If \(X\) is normal distributed \(X \sim N(0,1)\), its density is \(f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-x^2/2}\). Because it is symmetric, \(f_X(\sqrt{y}) = f_X(-\sqrt{y})\). We apply numbers on the above formula. \[f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} \left[ 2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(\sqrt{y})^2/2} \right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi y}} e^{-y/2}\] This is the Chi-square distribution with 1 degree of freedom.

If \(X \sim U(-1, 1)\) uniformly distributed. \(f_X(x) = \frac{1}{2}\) for \(x \in [-1, 1]\). So \(f_X(\sqrt{y}) = 1/2 = f_X(-\sqrt{y})\). Plugging these in: \[f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} \left[ \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \right] = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}\]

[Image of the PDF of Y = X^2 where X is Uniform between -1 and 1 showing the spike at 0]

3. Why the “Spike”? (Intuition)

Look at how \(X\) values map to \(Y\) values: * If \(X\) is between \(0\) and \(0.1\), its “width” is \(0.1\). * The resulting \(Y\) values (\(X^2\)) are between \(0\) and \(0.01\). Its “width” is now only \(0.01\).

The math of the “Clump”: We have “squeezed” a segment of width 0.1 into a tiny space of width 0.01. Because probability must be conserved (the area under the curve must stay the same), the density (height) must shoot up to compensate for the lost width.

As \(y\) approaches \(0\), the term \(\frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}\) approaches infinity. This is a “vertical asymptote” representing the fact that \(x^2\) stays near zero for a relatively long time compared to when \(x\) is near 1.

[Image comparing the uniform PDF of X and the transformed PDF of Y = X^2 to illustrate probability conservation]

4. Verification (Integration)

For \(f_Y(y)\) to be a valid PDF, its total area must be 1: \[\int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} dy = \left[ \sqrt{y} \right]_0^1 = 1 - 0 = 1\] The math holds up!

The density \(f_Y(y)\) is very high near \(0\). Because when you square numbers near zero (like \(0.1\) or \(-0.1\)), they “clump” together into even smaller numbers (\(0.01\)). This “clumping” effect creates the high density (the spike) near zero.
