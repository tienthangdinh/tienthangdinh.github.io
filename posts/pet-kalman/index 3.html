Kalman Filter

Start with Assumption about System Model

Before we calculate anything, we define how the world works using 2 things:

  • State Transition Equation: \(x_k = \Phi x_{k-1} + w_k\)

    • \(x_k\): The true state (e.g., position and velocity) at time \(k\).
    • \(\Phi\) (or \(F\)): The matrix that moves the state forward in time (the “physics”).
    • \(w_k\): Process Noise (variance \(Q\)) (like the wind?)

  • Observation Equation: \(z_k = H x_k + v_k\)

    • \(z_k\): The actual measurement from the sensor. (what sensor sees)
    • \(H\): The matrix that maps the state to what the sensor “sees.”
    • \(v_k\): Measurement Noise (variance \(R\)).

Step 1: Predict

Project the State:\[\hat{x}_{k|k-1} = \Phi \hat{x}_{k-1}\]

Project the Error Covariance:\[P_{k|k-1} = \Phi P_{k-1} \Phi^T + Q\]

Step 2: Update using Measurement

Calculate Kalman Gain (\(K\)): (how much we trust the sensor measurement = how much we untrust the prediction) \[K_k = P_{k|k-1} H^T (H P_{k|k-1} H^T + R)^{-1}\]

Update State with the trust of sensor measurement: \[\hat{x}_k = \hat{x}_{k|k-1} + K_k (z_k - H \hat{x}_{k|k-1})\]

Decrease the Uncertainty: \[P_k = (I - K_k H) P_{k|k-1}\]

FAQ

Why \(\Phi P_{k-1} \Phi^T\) for Project the Error Covariance?

Well it is called covariance propagation from current state \(x_{old}\) to next state \(x_{new}\). If \(x_{new} = \phi x_{old}\), then the variance \(P_{new} = \phi^2 P_{old}\).

Why \(H P H^T\) in the Kalman Gain?

Actually for exactly the same reason. Covariance propagation from the state space \(x\) to the measurement space \(z = Hx\). So there covariance has quadratic relationship \(P_z = H P H^T\)

Why $\(P H^T\) in the Kalman Gain?

Actually we are just applying the \(z = Hx\), we are just mapping the same \(P\) of \(x\) to \(z\) space. IT IS A MAPPING OF COVARIANCE; NOT A PROPAGATION OF COVARIANCE

Why \(K_k H\) in Uncertainty Update?

  • \(H\) takes your internal state (Position) and maps it to the sensor (Volts) (\(z_k = H x_k + v_k\)).
  • \(K\) takes that sensor information (Volts) and maps it back to your state (Position) (\(x_k = x_{k|k-1} + K_k (z_k - H x_{k|k-1})\)).
  • \(KH\) eventually bring back to a scaling scalar in sensor measurement space, it is the percentage of your current uncertainty that has been “explained” or “resolved” by the new measurement.

Example 1

System Setup (Model Assumption)

Imagine a car moving along a straight line. We check its status every \(\Delta t = 1\) second.

State Vector: \(\mathbf{x}_k = \begin{bmatrix} s_k \\ v_k \end{bmatrix}\) (Position, Velocity)

State Transition Matrix (\(\Phi\)) in \(x_k = \Phi x_{k-1} + w_k\): (\(s_{new} = s_{old} + v \cdot \Delta t\) and \(v_{new} = v_{old}\)): \[\Phi = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

Observation Matrix (\(H\)) in \(z_k = H x_k + v_k\): Our GPS measures directly only position, so 1 for that: \[H = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Noise: Let’s assume Measurement Noise \(R = 4\) and Process Noise \(Q = 0\) (perfect physics).

Initial Guess

\(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(P_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

Prediction Step (Time = 1s)

Before looking at the GPS, we predict: \[\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{1|0} = \Phi \mathbf{\hat{x}}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{\begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix}}\] \[P_{1|0} = \Phi P_0 \Phi^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

Update Step (Time = 1s)

Now we get the GPS measurement \(z_1 = 13\) (eventhough we predicted 10 :)))

Calculate Kalman Gain (trust of sensor measurement = how much we untrust the prediction): \[K_1 = P_{1|0} H^T (H P_{1|0} H^T + R)^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} (\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + 4)^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} (2 + 4)^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 2/6 \\ 1/6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.333 \\ 0.167 \end{bmatrix}\]

Update State with the trust of sensor measurement: \[\mathbf{\hat{x}}_1 = \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{1|0} + K_1 (z_1 - H \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{1|0}) = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0.333 \\ 0.167 \end{bmatrix} (13 - 10) = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{\begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 10.5 \end{bmatrix}}\]

Decrease the Uncertainty: \[P_1 = (I - K_1 H) P_{1|0} = (\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0.333 & 0 \\ 0.167 & 0 \end{bmatrix}) \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.667 & 1 \\ -0.167 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1.333 & 0.667 \\ 0.833 & 0.833 \end{bmatrix}\]

Scalar Examples

Simplest Static Example

Firstly the difference:

  • MSE says: “I used \(Y\) once in the lab to find \(a=1.214\). I will use \(1.214\) forever.”
  • Kalman says: “I used the statistics of \(Y\) (its variance) to set my initial trust. But as I see more \(X\) values, I will look at the ‘surprises’ (innovations) to refine my guess in real-time.”

The Static MSE Approach

You use the Ground Truth \(Y\) in the lab to find a fixed weight \(a\). You never change that weight and use it to estimate \(Y\) from \(X\) in real world.

What we know from the World Model: true temperature is constant \(x = 20\) (or manufacturer etc…)

Measurements: \(z_1 = 18\), \(z_2 = 22\), \(z_3 = 19\). We usually solve using \(C_{YX} = a C_X\). Now let’s calculate the Covariances (\(C_X\) and \(C_{YX}\)):

\[E\{X_1^2\} = \frac{18^2 + 22^2 + 19^2}{3} = \frac{1161}{3} = 387\] \[E\{Y X_1\} = \frac{(20\cdot18) + (20\cdot22) + (20\cdot19)}{3} = \frac{1180}{3}\]

Solution \(a = \frac{E\{YX_1\}}{E\{X_1^2\}} = \frac{1180/3}{387}\) \[\hat{Y} = \frac{1}{3}(a X_1 + a X_2 + a X_3) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1180}{387 \cdot 3} \cdot (18 + 22 + 19) \approx 19.9885\]

Static Kalman Filter

“I don’t know the truth \(Y\), but I will keep collecting data until trust in data \(P\) is more than trust in sensor noise \(\sigma_v^2\). Because it’s when we say ‘I trust my filter more than the next measurement’. And \(P_0\) is the initial trust. and \(\hat{x}_0\) is the initial guess.”

What we know from the World Model: this time not the value \(x=20\) but the statistics. Sensor with noise variance \(\sigma_v^2 = 4\).We start with a “Prior Belief” (initial guess) of \(\hat{x}_0 = 25\) with an initial uncertainty (error variance) of \(P_0 = 10\).

Step 1: \(z_1 = 18\): \[K_1 = \frac{P_0}{P_0 + \sigma_v^2} = \frac{10}{10 + 4} = \mathbf{0.714}\] \[\hat{x}_1 = \hat{x}_0 + K_1(z_1 - \hat{x}_0) = 25 + 0.714(18 - 25) = \mathbf{20}\] \[P_1 = (1 - K_1)P_0 = (1 - 0.714) \times 10 = \mathbf{2.86}\]

Step 2: \(z_2 = 22\): \[K_2 = \frac{P_1}{P_1 + \sigma_v^2} = \frac{2.86}{2.86 + 4} = \mathbf{0.417}\] \[\hat{x}_2 = \hat{x}_1 + K_2(z_2 - \hat{x}_1) = 20 + 0.417(22 - 20) = \mathbf{20.83}\] \[P_2 = (1 - K_2)P_1 = (1 - 0.417) \times 2.86 = \mathbf{1.67}\]

We keep measuring until \(P_n\) is smaller than sensor noise \(\sigma_v^2\). Because it’s when we say “I trust my filter more than the next measurement”.

Dynamic Example

Increasing temperature

  • True State (\(x_t\)): Starts at 20°C and increases: \(x_t = x_{t-1} + 1\).
  • Process Noise (\(Q\)): We are 100% sure about this “+1” rule, so \(Q = 0\).
  • Sensor Noise (\(\sigma_v^2\)): Still 4.
  • Initial Guess: \(\hat{x}_0 = 20\) with uncertainty \(P_0 = 2\).

Step 1: (Sensor measured \(z_1 = 22.5\)):

  • Predicted State: \(\hat{x}_{1|0} = 20 + 1 = \mathbf{21}\)
  • Predicted Uncertainty: \(P_{1|0} = P_0 + Q = 2 + 0 = \mathbf{2}\)
  • Kalman Gain (how much we trust the sensor): \(K_1 = \frac{P_{1|0}}{P_{1|0} + \sigma_v^2} = \frac{2}{2 + 4} = \mathbf{0.333}\)
  • Updated State: \(\hat{x}_1 = \hat{x}_{1|0} + K_1(z_1 - \hat{x}_{1|0}) = 21 + 0.333(22.5 - 21) = \mathbf{21.5}\)
  • Updated Uncertainty: \(P_1 = (1 - K_1)P_{1|0} = (1 - 0.333) \times 2 = \mathbf{1.334}\)

Step 2: (Sensor measured \(z_2 = 21\))

  • Predicted State: \(\hat{x}_{2|1} = \hat{x}_1 + 1 = 21.5 + 1 = \mathbf{22.5}\)
  • Predicted Uncertainty: \(P_{2|1} = P_1 + Q = 1.334 + 0 = \mathbf{1.334}\)
  • Kalman Gain: \(K_2 = \frac{P_{2|1}}{P_{2|1} + \sigma_v^2} = \frac{1.334}{1.334 + 4} = \mathbf{0.249}\)
  • Updated State: \(\hat{x}_2 = \hat{x}_{2|1} + K_2(z_2 - \hat{x}_{2|1}) = 22.5 + 0.249(21 - 22.5) = \mathbf{22.126}\)
  • Updated Uncertainty: \(P_2 = (1 - K_2)P_{2|1} = (1 - 0.249) \times 1.334 = \mathbf{1.002}\)
