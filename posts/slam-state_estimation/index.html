Formulation

In all other blogs, we usually use \(\mathbf{x}\) to denote only the camera pose. In SLAM, we don’t just want to know where we are; we want to know where everything else is too. We pack them all into one giant vector to optimize them together:\[\mathbf{x}_k \triangleq \{ \mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{y}_1, \dots, \mathbf{y}_m \} \tag{8.2}\]

Every SLAM system, whether it uses a Kalman Filter or Optimization, is built on these two pillars:

  • Prediction (Motion Model): \[\mathbf{x}_k = f(\mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k) + \mathbf{w}_k\]
  • Correction (Measurement Model): \[\mathbf{z}_k = h(\mathbf{x}_k) + \mathbf{v}_k\]

We want to find the probability distribution of the state \(\mathbf{x}_k\) given all our past inputs (\(\mathbf{u}\)) and observations (\(\mathbf{z}\)):\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k})\] In SLAM, we are always working with Conditional Bayes, where everything is already dependent on our “history” (let’s call the history \(H\)):\[P(A|B, H) = \frac{P(B|A, H) P(A|H)}{P(B|H)}\] We want the probability of our position using both measurement and history:\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{z}_k, H)\]

Using Conditiona Bayes to “flip” the problem between \(\mathbf{z}_k\) and \(\mathbf{x}_k\):\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{z}_k, H) = \frac{P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k, H) P(\mathbf{x}_k | H)}{P(\mathbf{z}_k | H)}\]

Look at the term \(P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k, H)\). The measurement, physics of camera etc are not dependent on our history. It is true that in BA, we maximize the samt \(P(\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{y}_1, \dots, \mathbf{y}_m | \mathbf{z}_{1:k})\), but inside each frame \(z_{i,j}\) only depends on \(x_i\) and \(y_j\) : \[P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k, H) \approx P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k)\]

The denominator \(P(\mathbf{z}_k | H)\) is just the probability of seeing those specific pixels regardless of where you are. Since it doesn’t depend on our variable \(\mathbf{x}_k\), we treat it as a constant:\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{z}_k, H) \propto P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k) P(\mathbf{x}_k | H)\] Which is: \[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \dots) \propto \underbrace{P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k)}_{\text{Likelihood}} \underbrace{P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1})}_{\text{Prior}}\]

  • Likelihood: “How well does my current guess explain the pixels I am seeing right now?” (reprojection/photometric error in previous blog).
  • Prior: “What did I think my position was before I opened my eyes in this frame, based on my previous movement?”. And the prior itself is also not sure: \[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) = \int P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) P(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) d\mathbf{x}_{k-1}\]

There are 2 approaches to solve this problem:

  • Filtering Method (EKF) faster but less accurate
  • Optimization Method (Batch/Nonlinear) slower but more accurate

Linear State Estimation - KF

Let’s look back at this: \[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) = \int P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) P(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) d\mathbf{x}_{k-1}\]

Simplifying the Motion Model: Markov property If we know the previous state \(\mathbf{x}_{k-1}\) and the current command \(\mathbf{u}_k\), the rest of the history is irrelevant:\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) = P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k)\]

Simplifying the Previous Distribution:The distribution at \(k-1\) doesn’t care about the command \(\mathbf{u}_k\) we just gave (because the command hasn’t happened yet in that timeframe):\[P(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) = P(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k-1}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1})\]

We assume the robot’s life follows these two linear equations:\[\begin{cases} \mathbf{x}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \mathbf{x}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k + \mathbf{w}_k \\ \mathbf{z}_k = \mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{v}_k \end{cases} \]

  • \(\mathbf{A}_k\): State transition matrix (how the robot moves).
  • \(\mathbf{C}_k\): Observation matrix (how the camera sees).
  • \(\mathbf{w}_k, \mathbf{v}_k\): Random noise.

We assume this noise is Zero-mean Gaussian:\[\mathbf{w}_k \sim N(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{R}), \quad \mathbf{v}_k \sim N(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{Q})\]

  • \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k\) (Up Hat): The Posterior. This is our best guess after looking at the camera image.
  • \(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k\) (Down Hat): The Prior. This is our best guess after moving, but before looking at the camera image.

Step 1: PREDICTION

We take our previous best guess (\(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}\) and its uncertainty \(\hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1}\)) and push it through the motion equation.The Probability Distribution:\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) = N(\mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k, \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1} \mathbf{A}_k^T + \mathbf{R})\]

The Practical Update Formulas:\[\check{\mathbf{x}}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k, \quad \check{\mathbf{P}}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1} \mathbf{A}_k^T + \mathbf{R}\]

Intuition: Notice that \(\check{\mathbf{P}}_k\) (the new uncertainty) is the old uncertainty plus \(\mathbf{R}\) (the motion noise). “The more you move without looking, the more lost you get.”

Then, we define what we expect to see. If we are at state \(\mathbf{x}_k\), our camera should see:\[P(\mathbf{z}_k | \mathbf{x}_k) = N(\mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{Q})\]

Step 2: UPDATE We have a Prior (our guess after moving) and a Likelihood (what the camera sees). Now, we multiply them together to get the Posterior (the final, best estimate). We want the posterior distribution \(N(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k, \hat{\mathbf{P}}_k)\). According to Bayes’ rule, this is the product of the likelihood and the prior:\[N(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k, \hat{\mathbf{P}}_k) = \eta N(\mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{Q}) \cdot N(\mathbf{x}_k, \check{\mathbf{P}}_k)\]\(\eta\): A normalization constant (to make sure the probability area sums to 1).

The Goal now: Find the new mean (\(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k\)) and new covariance (\(\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k\)).

The exponent of a Gaussian \(N(\mu, \Sigma)\) is \(-\frac{1}{2}(x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu)\). Since we are multiplying Gaussians, we add their exponents. To find the new parameters, we compare the coefficients of the quadratic terms:\[(\mathbf{x}_k - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k)^T \hat{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1} (\mathbf{x}_k - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k) = (\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{x}_k)^T \mathbf{Q}^{-1} (\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{x}_k) + (\mathbf{x}_k - \check{\mathbf{x}}_k)^T \check{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1} (\mathbf{x}_k - \check{\mathbf{x}}_k)\]

By looking at the terms containing \(\mathbf{x}_k^2\) (the second-order coefficients), we find the Information Matrix (the inverse of the covariance):\[\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1} = \mathbf{C}_k^T \mathbf{Q}^{-1} \mathbf{C}_k + \check{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1}\]

Intuition: Information adds up! The “sureness” of your final position is the “sureness” of your move plus the “sureness” of your camera. This is why the final \(\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k\) is always smaller (more certain) than either the prior or the likelihood alone.

To make this easier to compute, we defines the Kalman Gain (K):\[\mathbf{K} = \hat{\mathbf{P}}_k \mathbf{C}_k^T \mathbf{Q}^{-1}\]

Using some matrix algebra, we get the standard update for the covariance:\[\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K} \mathbf{C}_k) \check{\mathbf{P}}_k\]

By comparing the first-order coefficients (the terms with just \(\mathbf{x}_k\)), we find how the mean shifts:\[\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1} \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k = \mathbf{C}_k^T \mathbf{Q}^{-1} \mathbf{z}_k + \check{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1} \check{\mathbf{x}}_k\]

After substituting the Kalman Gain \(\mathbf{K}\) and rearranging, we reach the famous Kalman Filter update equation:\[\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k = \check{\mathbf{x}}_k + \mathbf{K}(\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{C}_k \check{\mathbf{x}}_k)\]

Explaination:

  • \((\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{C}_k \check{\mathbf{x}}_k)\): This is the Innovation (or Residual). it is the difference between what the camera actually saw (\(\mathbf{z}_k\)) and what we expected it to see (\(\mathbf{C}_k \check{\mathbf{x}}_k\)).
  • \(\mathbf{K}\): This is the Trust Factor.If the camera is very noisy (\(\mathbf{Q}\) is huge), \(\mathbf{K}\) becomes small. We ignore the camera and stay with our prior guess.If the camera is perfect (\(\mathbf{Q}\) is zero), \(\mathbf{K}\) becomes large. We ignore our prior and jump to whatever the camera says.
  • The Update: Your new position is your old guess + (The Kalman Gain \(\times\) The Prediction Error).
  • You might notice that \(\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1} = \mathbf{C}_k^T \mathbf{Q}^{-1} \mathbf{C}_k + \check{\mathbf{P}}_k^{-1}\) looks very similar to the \(\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}\) we saw in Gauss-Newton. The Kalman Filter is indeed doing one single step of Gauss-Newton every time a new image arrives.

Sum up Kalman Filter Algorithm

  1. Predict (Motion Update) We project our current state and uncertainty forward in time using the motion model. \[ \begin{aligned} \check{\mathbf{x}}_k &= \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k \\ \check{\mathbf{P}}_k &= \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1} \mathbf{A}_k^T + \mathbf{R} \end{aligned} \]

  2. Update (Measurement Update) First, we calculate the Kalman Gain (\(\mathbf{K}\)), which determines how much we trust the new sensor data versus our internal prediction: \[ \mathbf{K} = \check{\mathbf{P}}_k \mathbf{C}_k^T (\mathbf{C}_k \check{\mathbf{P}}_k \mathbf{C}_k^T + \mathbf{Q}_k)^{-1} \] Then, we calculate the Posterior state and covariance: \[ \begin{aligned} \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k &= \check{\mathbf{x}}_k + \mathbf{K}(\mathbf{z}_k - \mathbf{C}_k \check{\mathbf{x}}_k) \\ \hat{\mathbf{P}}_k &= (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K} \mathbf{C}_k) \check{\mathbf{P}}_k \end{aligned} \]

Explaination:

Your question would be, how did we come from \[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) = \int P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) P(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) d\mathbf{x}_{k-1}\] to \[\begin{cases} \mathbf{x}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \mathbf{x}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k + \mathbf{w}_k \\ \mathbf{z}_k = \mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{v}_k \end{cases} \]

Firstly, \(\mathbf{x}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \mathbf{x}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k + \mathbf{w}_k\) is the probability \(P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k)\).

  • The Mean of the probability is \(\mathbf{A}_k \mathbf{x}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k\) (where we expect to land).
  • The Variance (the spread of the cloud) is determined by the noise term \(\mathbf{w}_k\).

Secondly, The term \(P(\mathbf{x}_{k-1} | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k-1}, \mathbf{z}_{1:k-1}) d\mathbf{x}_{k-1}\) represents all the result points of the previous frame. It is the bell curve (Gaussian) that the robot calculated at time \(k-1\). And you know, we dont save all points like that, we summarize them into two variables:

  • \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}\): The mean (the center of the earlier point).
  • \(\hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1}\): The covariance (how dense the distribution is).

AND EVENTUALLY, both things above are collapsed into this:

  • The center of the distribution: \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}\) (in: \(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1} + \mathbf{u}_k\) )
  • The width of the distribution: \(\hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1}\) (in: \(\check{\mathbf{P}}_k = \mathbf{A}_k \hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1} \mathbf{A}_k^T + \mathbf{R}\) )

Non-linear Kalman Filter (EKF)

But u know, in reality, camera projections (division by \(Z\)) and rotations (\(SE(3)\) or Quaternions) are “curvy” functions. If you push Gaussian bell curve through a “curvy” function, the output is no longer Gaussian. Since the Kalman Filter only knows how to handle bell curves, we have a problem. To keep the math fast and “Gaussian,” the EKF uses a trick: Taylor Expansion. We pretend the function is a straight line, but only for a tiny, local area around our current guess.

Instead of the simple \(\mathbf{A}x\) and \(\mathbf{C}x\), we now have general nonlinear functions:

  • Motion: \(\mathbf{x}_k = f(\mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k) + \mathbf{w}_k\)
  • Observation: \(\mathbf{z}_k = h(\mathbf{x}_k) + \mathbf{n}_k\)

To make these “linear,” we perform a First-order Taylor Expansion at our current best estimate. And you know, first order is always linear.

The Motion Model Approximation We expand around the previous posterior \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}\):\[\mathbf{x}_k \approx f(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}_{k-1}}\bigg|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}} (\mathbf{x}_{k-1} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}) + \mathbf{w}_k\] \[\mathbf{x}_k \approx \underbrace{f(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k)}_{\text{Center Move}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{F} \cdot (\text{Deviation from Center})}_{\text{Cloud Stretching}}\]

\((\mathbf{x}_{k-1} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1})\) is the “Error” or the “Deviation” from our mean. Our robot thinks it is at the center of a bell curve (\(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}\)). But the bell curve has “width.” in different direction. The term \((\mathbf{x}_{k-1} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1})\) represents any random point inside that cloud relative to the center. So yes, we take the “width” of our old uncertainty and multiply it by the slope (\(\mathbf{F}\)) to see how much the cloud “stretches” or “squashes” during the move.

Jacobian Matrix for \(f\) is \(\mathbf{F}\). It now plays the role of the matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) from the linear version. \[\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}_{k-1}}\bigg|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}}\]

The Observation Model Approximation Exactly the same way, we expand the camera’s observation function around our predicted state \(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k\):\[\mathbf{z}_k \approx h(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k) + \frac{\partial h}{\partial \mathbf{x}_k}\bigg|_{\check{\mathbf{x}}_k} (\mathbf{x}_k - \check{\mathbf{x}}_k) + \mathbf{n}_k\]

Just like we found the slope of the motion (\(\mathbf{F}\)), we now find the slope of the camera observation function \(h\) at our predicted position \(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k\):\[\mathbf{H} = \frac{\partial h}{\partial \mathbf{x}_k} \bigg|_{\check{\mathbf{x}}_k}\]

Remember that giant \(2 \times 6\) matrix we talked about in the Direct Method in an earlier blog?\(\mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \delta \xi}\). That IS the Jacobian for the observation model \(H\), you are literally plugging in those geometric derivatives.

1. Prediction We move the center of our distribution through the “curvy” reality, but we stretch the uncertainty as if it were a straight line:\[P(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_{1:k}, \mathbf{z}_{0:k-1}) = N(f(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k), \mathbf{F}\hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1}\mathbf{F}^T + \mathbf{R}_k)\]

In practical terms:

  • Predicted Mean: \(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k = f(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k)\)
  • Predicted Covariance: \(\check{\mathbf{P}}_k = \mathbf{F}\hat{\mathbf{P}}_{k-1}\mathbf{F}^T + \mathbf{R}_k\)

2. Update Now we “open our eyes” and look at the camera image \(\mathbf{z}_k\). We calculate the Kalman Gain, substituting our Jacobian \(\mathbf{H}\) for the old linear matrix \(\mathbf{C}\):\[\mathbf{K}_k = \check{\mathbf{P}}_k \mathbf{H}^T (\mathbf{H}\check{\mathbf{P}}_k \mathbf{H}^T + \mathbf{Q}_k)^{-1}\]

Finally, we update our guess to get the Posterior: \[\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k = \check{\mathbf{x}}_k + \mathbf{K}_k(\mathbf{z}_k - h(\check{\mathbf{x}}_k))\] \[\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k \mathbf{H}) \check{\mathbf{P}}_k\]

Observation: * We use the linear Jacobian \(\mathbf{H}\) to decide how much to trust the sensor. * But we use the nonlinear projection \(h\) to calculate the actual error. * This “hybrid” approach helps the EKF stay much more accurate than if it were purely linear.

Bundle Adjustment

While the EKF (from the previous section) tries to “summarize” the past into a single state, BA keeps all the original data and wiggles everything—poses and points—until they fit together perfectly. Let’s see how it works.

Camera Projection Model

To formulate error,let’s start with a 3D point \(\mathbf{p}\) becomes a 2D pixel \(\mathbf{z}\). This is the “Observation Equation” \(h(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\) broken down into its physics steps.

Step 1: World to Camera Frame Transform the 3D point \(\mathbf{p}\) using the camera’s rotation \(\mathbf{R}\) and translation \(\mathbf{t}\):\[\mathbf{P}' = \mathbf{R}\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{t} = [X', Y', Z']^T\]

Step 2: Projection to Normalized PlaneDivide by depth (\(Z'\)) to get coordinates on the virtual plane 1 meter away from the lens:\[\mathbf{P}_c = [u_c, v_c, 1]^T = [X'/Z', Y'/Z', 1]^T\]

Step 3: Distortion Distortion coefficients \(k_1, k_2\) (Note: \(r_c^2 = u_c^2 + v_c^2\) is the squared distance from the center).:\[\begin{cases} u'_c = u_c(1 + k_1r_c^2 + k_2r_c^4) \\ v'_c = v_c(1 + k_1r_c^2 + k_2r_c^4) \end{cases}\]

Step 4: Intrinsics to Pixel Coordinates Use the focal lengths (\(f_x, f_y\)) and principal point (\(c_x, c_y\)) to find the final pixel address:\[\begin{cases} u_s = f_x u'_c + c_x \\ v_s = f_y v'_c + c_y \end{cases}\]

Summarize: If we group all the camera parameters (like pose \(\mathbf{T}\) and intrinsics) as \(\mathbf{x}\) and the 3D landmark positions as \(\mathbf{y}\), we can summarize all four steps above into one function \(h\):\[\mathbf{z} = h(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\]

In a perfect world, your camera would see the landmark exactly where \(h(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\) says it should be. In reality, noise makes them different. We define the Reprojection Error (\(\mathbf{e}\)):\[\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{z} - h(\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{p})\]

Our goal is to minimize the sum of all squared errors:\[\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{j=1}^n \|\mathbf{e}_{ij}\|^2 = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{j=1}^n \|\mathbf{z}_{ij} - h(\mathbf{T}_i, \mathbf{p}_j)\|^2\]

Solve BA

In BA, our unknown variable \(\mathbf{x}\) is a giant vector containing every camera pose and every 3D landmark.\[\mathbf{x} = [\mathbf{T}_1, \dots, \mathbf{T}_m, \mathbf{p}_1, \dots, \mathbf{p}_n]^T\]

To solve this using Gauss-Newton, we need to find an increment \(\Delta \mathbf{x}\) that reduces the error. We split this increment into two parts:

  • \(\Delta \mathbf{x}_c\): The update for all \(m\) camera poses (in Lie Algebra \(\mathfrak{se}(3)\)).
  • \(\Delta \mathbf{x}_p\): The update for all \(n\) 3D point positions.

The cost function is the sum of all reprojection errors. We use a Taylor expansion to linearize it. For a single observation of point \(j\) by camera \(i\), the error becomes:\[\frac{1}{2} \|f(\mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x})\|^2 \approx \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{j=1}^n \|\mathbf{e}_{ij} + \mathbf{F}_{ij}\Delta\xi_i + \mathbf{E}_{ij}\Delta\mathbf{p}_j\|^2\]

  • \(\mathbf{F}_{ij}\): “If I wiggle the \(i\)-th camera pose (\(\delta\xi_i\)), how much does the \(j\)-th pixel move?”
  • \(\mathbf{E}_{ij}\): “If I wiggle the \(j\)-th 3D point (\(\Delta \mathbf{p}_j\)), how much does its projection move on the \(i\)-th camera’s sensor?”

When we stack all these observations into one large matrix, the equation simplifies to:\[\frac{1}{2} \|f(\mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x})\|^2 = \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{e} + \mathbf{F}\Delta\mathbf{x}_c + \mathbf{E}\Delta\mathbf{x}_p\|^2\]

To find the best Gradient and \(\Delta\mathbf{x}\), we solve the linear system derived from the Gauss-Newton or Levenberg-Marquardt method:\[\mathbf{H}\Delta\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{g}\]

  • \(\mathbf{H}\): The Hessian matrix (Information Matrix).
  • \(\mathbf{g}\): The Gradient vector.

Since we categorized the variables into poses and landmarks. We define the global Jacobian \(\mathbf{J}\) by placing the camera Jacobians and point Jacobians side-by-side:\[\mathbf{J} = [\mathbf{F} \quad \mathbf{E}]\] \[\mathbf{J} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} \mathbf{F}_{11} & \mathbf{0} & \dots & \mathbf{E}_{11} & \mathbf{0} & \dots \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{21} & \dots & \mathbf{E}_{21} & \dots & \dots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{array} \right]\]

The Hessian matrix \(\mathbf{H}\) is built by multiplying \(\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}\). This results in a very specific block-matrix structure:\[\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{F}^T\mathbf{F} & \mathbf{F}^T\mathbf{E} \\ \mathbf{E}^T\mathbf{F} & \mathbf{E}^T\mathbf{E} \end{bmatrix}\]

  • \(\mathbf{F}^T\mathbf{F}\): Interactions between cameras.
  • \(\mathbf{E}^T\mathbf{E}\): Interactions between landmarks.
  • The Off-Diagonals: The constraints that connect cameras to landmarks.

You know, H is a huge huge huge matrix. But it is mostly sparse, so in next chapter we solve uisng Schur Complement.

More explaination on E and F

Derivation

Main idea, we need to see how the error \(\mathbf{e}\) changes when we wiggle the landmark \(\mathbf{p}\) or the camera pose \(\mathbf{T}\).Let’s break down the projection \(h(\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{p})\) into two main layers:

  • Geometry Layer: The 3D point in world coordinates \(\mathbf{p}\) is moved to the camera frame \(\mathbf{P}' = [X', Y', Z']^T\).
  • Projection Layer: The camera-frame point \(\mathbf{P}'\) is projected into pixel coordinates \(\mathbf{u} = [u, v]^T\).

The Common Starting Component

\(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'}\)Both \(\mathbf{F}\) and \(\mathbf{E}\) share a middle step: How do pixel coordinates change if the 3D point inside the camera frame moves?From the projection equations \(u = f_x \frac{X'}{Z'} + c_x\) and \(v = f_y \frac{Y'}{Z'} + c_y\), we take the partial derivatives with respect to \(X', Y', Z'\):\[\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial X'} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial Y'} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial Z'} \\ \frac{\partial v}{\partial X'} & \frac{\partial v}{\partial Y'} & \frac{\partial v}{\partial Z'} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{f_x}{Z'} & 0 & -\frac{f_x X'}{Z'^2} \\ 0 & \frac{f_y}{Z'} & -\frac{f_y Y'}{Z'^2} \end{bmatrix}\]

Deriving \(\mathbf{E}\) (Landmark Jacobian)

\(\mathbf{E}\) is the derivative of the error with respect to the 3D world point \(\mathbf{p}\).Since \(\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{z} - h(\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{p})\), the Jacobian is \(-\frac{\partial h}{\partial \mathbf{p}}\).The Chain Rule:\[\mathbf{E} = - \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{P}'}{\partial \mathbf{p}}\]

We already have the first part. For the second part, since \(\mathbf{P}' = \mathbf{R}\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{t}\), the derivative with respect to \(\mathbf{p}\) is simply the rotation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\).The Final Result for \(\mathbf{E}\) has size \(2 \times 3\):\[\mathbf{E} = - \begin{bmatrix} \frac{f_x}{Z'} & 0 & -\frac{f_x X'}{Z'^2} \\ 0 & \frac{f_y}{Z'} & -\frac{f_y Y'}{Z'^2} \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{R}\]

Deriving \(\mathbf{F}\) (Camera Jacobian)

\(\mathbf{F}\) is the derivative of the error with respect to the camera pose perturbation \(\delta\xi \in \mathfrak{se}(3)\).The Chain Rule:\[\mathbf{F} = - \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{P}'}{\partial \delta \xi}\]

From Lie Algebra, we know that if we apply a tiny left-multiplication perturbation \(\exp(\delta\xi^\wedge)\) to the transformation, the derivative of the transformed point \(\mathbf{P}'\) with respect to the perturbation is:\[\frac{\partial \mathbf{P}'}{\partial \delta \xi} = [\mathbf{I} \quad -\mathbf{P}'^\wedge] = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & Z' & -Y' \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -Z' & 0 & X' \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & Y' & -X' & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]Now, multiply the two matrices together:\[\mathbf{F} = - \begin{bmatrix} \frac{f_x}{Z'} & 0 & -\frac{f_x X'}{Z'^2} \\ 0 & \frac{f_y}{Z'} & -\frac{f_y Y'}{Z'^2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & Z' & -Y' \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -Z' & 0 & X' \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & Y' & -X' & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]The Final Result for \(\mathbf{F}\):After performing the matrix multiplication, you get the \(2 \times 6\) matrix:\[\mathbf{F} = - \begin{bmatrix} \frac{f_x}{Z'} & 0 & -\frac{f_x X'}{Z'^2} & -\frac{f_x X' Y'}{Z'^2} & f_x + \frac{f_x X'^2}{Z'^2} & -\frac{f_x Y'}{Z'} \\ 0 & \frac{f_y}{Z'} & -\frac{f_y Y'}{Z'^2} & -f_y - \frac{f_y Y'^2}{Z'^2} & \frac{f_y X' Y'}{Z'^2} & \frac{f_y X'}{Z'} \end{bmatrix}\]

Example

  • Camera Intrinsics (\(\mathbf{K}\)): \(f_x = 500, f_y = 500, c_x = 320, c_y = 240\).
  • 3D Point (\(\mathbf{p}\)): \([1, 1, 5]^T\) in the camera frame. (So \(X'=1, Y'=1, Z'=5\)).
  • Camera Pose (\(\mathbf{T}\)): For simplicity, we’ll assume the camera is at the world origin with no rotation. (So \(\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{I}\) and \(\mathbf{t} = [0,0,0]^T\)).

Step 1: The Intermediate Projection Jacobian

First, we calculate \(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'}\). This tells us how the pixels \((u, v)\) react to 3D movement in the camera frame.Using the formulas:

  • \(\frac{f_x}{Z'} = \frac{500}{5} = \mathbf{100}\)
  • \(-\frac{f_x X'}{Z'^2} = -\frac{500 \cdot 1}{25} = \mathbf{-20}\)
  • \(\frac{f_y}{Z'} = \frac{500}{5} = \mathbf{100}\)
  • \(-\frac{f_y Y'}{Z'^2} = -\frac{500 \cdot 1}{25} = \mathbf{-20}\) \[\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'} = \begin{bmatrix} 100 & 0 & -20 \\ 0 & 100 & -20 \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 3: Calculate the Camera Jacobian (\(\mathbf{F}\))

We use the formula \(\mathbf{F} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{P}'} [\mathbf{I}, -\mathbf{P}'^\wedge]\).First, let’s find the skew-symmetric matrix \(-\mathbf{P}'^\wedge\):\[-\mathbf{P}'^\wedge = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & Z' & -Y' \\ -Z' & 0 & X' \\ Y' & -X' & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 5 & -1 \\ -5 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

Now we multiply: \[\mathbf{F} = - \begin{bmatrix} 100 & 0 & -20 \\ 0 & 100 & -20 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 5 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -5 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

After the matrix multiplication, we get: \[\mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix} -100 & 0 & 20 & 20 & -520 & 100 \\ 0 & -100 & 20 & 520 & -20 & -100 \end{bmatrix}\]

The first 3 columns are translation. The last 3 are rotation. Notice how rotation (\(520\)) has a much bigger impact on the pixels than translation (\(-100\))? This is why camera “shake” ruins photos so easily!

Step 4: Putting them together

\[\mathbf{J} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} \mathbf{F}_{11} & \mathbf{0} & \dots & \mathbf{E}_{11} & \mathbf{0} & \dots \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{21} & \dots & \mathbf{E}_{21} & \dots & \dots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{array} \right]\]

Setup

  • Cameras (\(m=3\)): Each has 6 degrees of freedom (Lie Algebra \(\xi \in \mathfrak{se}(3)\)).
  • Points (\(n=5\)): Each has 3 degrees of freedom (\(X, Y, Z\)).
  • Visibility: Let’s assume a realistic scenario where not every camera sees every point:
    • Cam 1 sees Points 1, 2, 3. (3 observations)
    • Cam 2 sees Points 2, 3, 4. (3 observations)
    • Cam 3 sees Points 3, 4, 5. (3 observations)
  • Total Observations: 9 pairs of (Camera \(i\), Point \(j\)).

Dimensions of the “Mega” Matrix \(J\)

The columns correspond to our variables. We have a “Camera section” and a “Landmark section.” * Camera Columns: \(3 \text{ cameras} \times 6 \text{ DOF} = \mathbf{18 \text{ columns}}\). * Landmark Columns: \(5 \text{ points} \times 3 \text{ DOF} = \mathbf{15 \text{ columns}}\). * Total Width of \(J\): 33 columns.

The rows correspond to our errors. Each observation \(z_{ij}\) produces a 2D residual (error in \(u\) and error in \(v\)). * Total Rows: \(9 \text{ observations} \times 2 \text{ residuals} = \mathbf{18 \text{ rows}}\).

=> Total Size of \(J\) for this example: \(18 \times 33\).

The Global Jacobian \(\mathbf{J}\)

This matrix has 18 rows (9 observations \(\times\) 2 pixels) and 33 columns (18 for poses + 15 for points). \[\mathbf{J} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|ccccc} \mathbf{F}_{11} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{11} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{F}_{12} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{12} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{F}_{13} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{13} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \hline \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{22} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{22} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{23} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{23} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{24} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{24} & \mathbf{0} \\ \hline \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{33} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{33} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{34} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{34} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{F}_{35} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{E}_{35} \end{array} \right]\]

Pixel resolution does not change anything. It is only about the number of features detected, and the number of camera poses.
