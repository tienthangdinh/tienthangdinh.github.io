The Rendering Equation

Basic

\[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\] whereby:

  • \(\cos\theta^{\text{in}}\): Lambertian cosine term. \(\theta^{\text{in}}\) is the angle between the incoming direction \(\omega^{\text{in}}\) and the surface normal \(\mathbf{n}\). The more senkrecht, the more energy reflected!

Official rendering equation: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) \mathbf{L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\text{trace}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}), -\omega^{\text{in}})} \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\]

And you know what is important? We have proved this: \[d\Omega^{\text{in}} = \sin\theta^{\text{in}} d\theta^{\text{in}} d\phi\]

Area-Based Rendering Equation: \[\Delta\Omega_x = \frac{\cos\theta^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}}{||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}||^2} \Delta A_y\] \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + \iint_{\mathbf{y} \in A} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{y}, \omega^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}) \mathbf{V(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})} \frac{\cos\theta^{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{y}} \cos\theta^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}}{||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}||^2} d A_y\]

Full Geometry Term: \[G(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{V(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})}\frac{\cos\theta^{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{y}} \cos\theta^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}}{||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}||^2}\]

Recursive Operator Form of the Rendering Equation

\[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T} \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}\]

  • \(L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}\): The total outgoing radiance (the unknown we want to solve for).
  • \(L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}\): The emitted light (the light sources).
  • \(\mathbf{T}: L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\text{trace}(\mathbf{x}, \omega), -\omega)\)
  • \(\mathbf{R}: L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\)
  • \(\mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T}\): The combined light transport operator. It takes light that is already outgoing from a surface (\(L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}\)), transports it to the next surface (\(T \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}\)), and reflects it (\(R \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}\)).

But how to solve this equation? \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T})^{-1} L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}\] Using Taylor series expansion: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R} \mathbf{T} \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T})^2 \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T})^3 \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \dots\]

where \(\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T}\) means reflected once, \((\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T})^2\) means reflected twice, etc.

Approaches to solve rendering equation

Radiosity

Idea: All surfaces are diffuse => from Integral Equation (solved by Monte Carlo) to just a Linear Equation \(Ax=b\) solved by matrix inversion.

\[E_{\lambda, i}^{\text{out}} = E_{\lambda, i}^{\text{emit}} + \sum_{j} \rho_i F_{i \to j} E_{\lambda, j}^{\text{out}}\] where:

  • \(\rho_i\): The albedo of surface \(i\).
  • \(F_{i \to j}\): The form factor (geometric term) from surface \(i\) to surface \(j\).

Example

Imagine 4 patches forming a small square “room”:

  • Patch 1 (Top): A light source. \(E_{1}^{\text{emit}} = 100\), \(\rho_1 = 0\) (absorbing).
  • Patch 2 (Bottom): A reflective floor. \(E_{2}^{\text{emit}} = 0\), \(\rho_2 = 0.8\).
  • Patch 3 (Left): A wall. \(E_{3}^{\text{emit}} = 0\), \(\rho_3 = 0.5\).
  • Patch 4 (Right): A wall. \(E_{4}^{\text{emit}} = 0\), \(\rho_4 = 0.5\).

The Form Factors (\(F_{i \to j}\)) Assume the geometry is such that patches see each other with the following visibility:

  • Opposite patches (1 to 2, 3 to 4) have \(F = 0.6\).
  • Adjacent patches (1 to 3, 1 to 4, etc.) have \(F = 0.2\).
  • Patches cannot see themselves, so \(F_{ii} = 0\).

Step 1: Set up the Equations The radiosity equation for each patch is: \(E_i = E_i^{\text{emit}} + \rho_i \sum (F_{ij} E_j)\).

  • \(E_1\): \(100 + 0 = \mathbf{100}\) (Since \(\rho_1 = 0\), it only emits).
  • \(E_2\): \(0 + 0.8 \cdot (0.6 E_1 + 0.2 E_3 + 0.2 E_4)\)
  • \(E_3\): \(0 + 0.5 \cdot (0.2 E_1 + 0.2 E_2 + 0.6 E_4)\)
  • \(E_4\): \(0 + 0.5 \cdot (0.2 E_1 + 0.2 E_2 + 0.6 E_3)\)

Step 2: Solve Iteratively (Gathering) Because these equations are interdependent (e.g., \(E_3\) depends on \(E_4\), and \(E_4\) depends on \(E_3\)), we solve them in “bounces”.

  • Iteration 1 (Direct Light only)

We plug \(E_1 = 100\) into the others, assuming \(E_{2,3,4} = 0\) initially:

  • \(E_2\): \(0.8 \cdot (0.6 \cdot 100) = \mathbf{48.0}\)

  • \(E_3\): \(0.5 \cdot (0.2 \cdot 100) = \mathbf{10.0}\)

  • \(E_4\): \(0.5 \cdot (0.2 \cdot 100) = \mathbf{10.0}\)

  • Iteration 2 (First Reflection/Bounce)

Now we plug the values from Iteration 1 back into the equations to see how light bounces between the walls:

  • \(E_2\): \(0.8 \cdot (60 + 0.2 \cdot 10 + 0.2 \cdot 10) = 0.8 \cdot (64) = \mathbf{51.2}\)
  • \(E_3\): \(0.5 \cdot (20 + 0.2 \cdot 48 + 0.6 \cdot 10) = 0.5 \cdot (20 + 9.6 + 6) = \mathbf{17.8}\)
  • \(E_4\): \(0.5 \cdot (20 + 0.2 \cdot 48 + 0.6 \cdot 10) = 0.5 \cdot (20 + 9.6 + 6) = \mathbf{17.8}\)

Step 3: Final Results If you continue this for 4–5 iterations, the numbers will stop changing (converge).

  • Patch 1: 100 (The Sun)
  • Patch 2: \(\approx 54\) (Bright floor)
  • Patch 3 & 4: \(\approx 22\) (Dimmer walls)

Still this example, but what if not diffuse anymore?

Imagine Patch 2 is a mirror. Now, instead of one value for \(E_2\), you need an infinite number of values for every possible direction \(\omega\). So for every point, we have this directional-based integral again: \[L_o(x, \omega_o) = L_e(x, \omega_o) + \int_{\Omega} \rho(x, \omega_i, \omega_o) L_i(x, \omega_i) \cos \theta_i d\omega_i\]

So what change? For example:

  • Diffuse Rule: Light from the Bulb (1) hits the Floor (2) and splits 20% to Wall 3 and 20% to Wall 4.
  • Glossy Rule: Light from the Bulb (1) hits the Floor (2). Because of the angle, 90% of that reflected light is directed at Wall 4, and 0% goes to Wall 3.

So we dont have that 20% \(\rho\) for every direction anymore, we have \(\rho(x, \omega_i, \omega_o)\) always varying. We have to use Monte Carlo to sample the direction.

Bidirectional Path Tracing (BDPT)

Path Sampling both Direction is a Monte Carlo Framework

We want to build this path: \(y_0 \to \dots \to y_j \to x_i \to \dots \to x_1\). There are so many thing that we randomly sample:

  • Random point on light source
  • Random pixel
  • Random direction (BRDF)
  • Russian Roulette at each bounce

Each connection is a sample of the total light transport, so we Monte Carlo it: \[F \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{f(x_i)}{p(x_i)}\]

So what is Multi Importance Sampling (MIS) in BDPT?

Using your 4-patch example (Light, Floor, Walls), imagine a path with 3 segments: Light \(\to\) Floor \(\to\) Wall \(\to\) Eye. This path can be generated by:

  • Tracing 3 steps from the light and 0 from the eye.
  • Tracing 2 steps from the light and 1 from the eye.
  • Tracing 1 step from the light and 2 from the eye.

Each of these is a different sampling strategy for the exact same physical path. If you just added them together, the image would be far too bright and physically incorrect.

MIS assigns a weight (\(w\)) to each strategy so that the total contribution equals 1. It follows the Balance Heuristic: \[w_i(P) = \frac{p_i(P)}{\sum_{j} p_j(P)}\]

\(p_i(P)\): The probability of generating path \(P\) using strategy \(i\).

But how to assign those probabilities?

  1. Are probabilities for the Path or the Segment?

The probability \(p\) used in MIS is for the entire path. \[p(\text{Path}) = p(x_0) \cdot p(x_1|x_0) \cdot p(x_2|x_1) \dots\]

  1. How are \(p_A\) and \(p_B\) calculated automatically?

To calculate the PDF for a strategy, the computer looks at the sampling rules used during the Monte Carlo process.

  • Strategy A (Camera-heavy): \(p_A\) = (Pixel PDF) \(\times\) (BRDF PDF) \(\times\) (Geometry Factor).

  • Strategy B (Light-heavy): \(p_B\) = (Light Area PDF) \(\times\) (Emission PDF) \(\times\) (BRDF Reflection PDF).

Once the computer has these two numbers (\(p_A\) and \(p_B\)), it uses the Balance Heuristic automatically: \[w_B = \frac{p_B}{p_A + p_B}\]

If \(p_B\) is 1000 and \(p_A\) is 0.001 (because hitting a tiny bulb is hard), then \(w_B\) becomes 0.9999.The computer doesn’t “know” it’s a glossy floor; it just sees that the math for Strategy B resulted in a much higher probability density for that specific path than Strategy A did.

More about Monte Carlo Philosophy

We all know that the rendering equation has 2 infinities: infinite dimensions and infinite samples: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T})^{-1} L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R}\mathbf{T} \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R}\mathbf{T})^2 \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \dots \quad \text{}\] This is a \(2 \times 2 + 2 \times 2 + 2 \times 2 + \dots = \infty\) dimensional integral. It is not like the same BRDF, here we zweig the directions too. So we still sum up, but using Monte Carlo philosophy, using importance sampling to approximate the integral.

For normal integration, error grows with the number of dimensions. Remember the triangles? yes! => Let’s dig in Monte Carlo instead.

Measurement Equation

Imagine exactly like a camera model. \(j\) is a pixel the image plane in the back. In front of the image plane is a hole/apeture of size \(A\), and \(x\) is a point on that hole/apeture.

The brightness (\(\Phi_{\lambda,j}\)) of a pixel \(j\): \[\Phi_{\lambda,j} = \iint_{x \in A} \iint_{\Omega_j^{in}(x)} W_\lambda^e(x, \omega^{in}) L_\lambda^{in}(x, \omega^{in}) \cos \theta^{in} d\Omega_j^{in} dA\]

  • It integrates all incoming radiance (\(L_\lambda^{in}\)) over each point \(x\) on the apeture \(A\), and each direction \(\omega^{in}\) that the light can come from. Some of the direction \(\omega^{in}\) going through point \(x\) will not point to pixel \(j\). But some will, we call this \(W_\lambda^e(x, \omega^{in})\) as follow. this part is the most confusing part for me until now.
  • If the ray coming from direction \(\omega^{\text{in}}\) at point \(\mathbf{x}\) hits the target pixel \(j\), then:\[W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = 1\]
  • If the ray misses pixel \(j\) or the entire sensor, then:\[W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = 0\]

Importance Function

Trace back a single race and see how much influence it has, 40% or 60%?… \[W_\lambda^{in}(x, \omega^{in}) = W_\lambda^e(x, \omega^{in}) + \iint_{\Omega^{out}} \rho(x, \omega^{out}, \omega^{in}) W_\lambda^{in}(trace(x, \omega^{out}), -\omega^{out}) \cos \theta^{out} d\Omega^{out}\]

How to use this? Let’s make a simplified example. We have two surfaces, A and B. We want to find the Incoming Importance (\(W^{\text{in}}\)) at a ray arriving at surface B from a light source.

  • Pixel (\(\mathbf{x}_0\)) has Sensor Efficiency \(W^e = W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = 1.0\) (Max influence)
  • Surface A (\(\mathbf{x}_1\)) has BRDF \(\rho_{A} = 0.5\) (Reflects 50% of influence)
  • Surface B (\(\mathbf{x}_2\)) has BRDF \(\rho_{B} = 0.8\) (Reflects 80% of influence)

Now we start at the sensor: \[W_{\lambda}^{in}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = W^{\text{in}}_{\text{Pixel}} = W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) + 0 = 1.0 + 0 = 1.0\]

That means influence leaving the pixel is \(W^{\text{out}}_{\text{Pixel}} = 1.0\).

Now we go to surface A. Surface A is the first surface hit by the ray traced backward from the pixel. Outgoing Importance \(W^{\text{out}}_{\text{A}}\) (Toward the Pixel): \[W^{\text{out}}_{\text{A} \to \text{Pixel}} = W^{\text{in}}_{\text{Pixel}} = 1.0\]

That means Influence leaving A toward the pixel = Influence arriving at the pixel from A.

Now we see what is the incoming importance at A- \(W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}}\): \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}} \approx \rho_{A} \cdot W^{\text{out}}_{\text{A} \to \text{Pixel}}\] \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}} = 0.5 \cdot 1.0 = 0.5\] That means a photon arriving at A only has \(50\%\) of the initial pixel’s influence, because A is a \(50\%\) reflector.

Surface B is hit after the ray scatters from A. Influence leaving B toward A = Influence arriving at A from B. \[W^{\text{out}}_{\text{B} \to \text{A}} = W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}} = 0.5\]

Now Incoming Importance (\(W^{\text{in}}\)) at B. (which flows back to the light source): \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{B}} \approx \rho_{B} \cdot W^{\text{out}}_{\text{B} \to \text{A}}\] \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{B}} = 0.8 \cdot 0.5 = 0.4\]

Conclusion: The influence of \(1.0\) started at the pixel, traveled backward, lost \(50\%\) at surface A, and lost \(20\%\) at surface B, resulting in an overall accumulated influence of \(0.4\) for the entire path. The renderer now knows that if it finds a light path ending at B, that light must be weighted by \(0.4\) to contribute correctly to the pixel.

Radiometry

Let’s just start this simple, each chronomotic light in a interval of time \(t\) brings some kind of power: \[\Phi_{\lambda,t} = \frac{dQ{\lambda}}{dt} \quad (W)\]

Irradiance is how much of that power in an area from ALL DIRECTIONS: \[H_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{d\Phi_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}}{dA} \quad (W/m^2)\]

Radiance is how much of that power IN 1 SPECIFIC DIRECTION, REMEMBER ONLY DIRECTION, AREA DOESNT MATTER: \[L_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) = \frac{d^2\Phi_{\lambda}}{dA_{\perp} d\Omega} \quad (\text{W} \cdot \text{m}^{-2} \cdot \text{sr}^{-1})\]

Since the area in radiance does not matter, if we want to convert into irradiance, \(dA = \frac{dA_{\perp}}{\cos\theta}\).

So we could see direct relationship between irradiance and radiance: \[H_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}) = \iint_{\Omega} L_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) \cos\theta d\Omega\]

BRDF: \[\rho_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = \frac{dL_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}})}{dH_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}})}\]

whereby: \(dH_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\)

Hemispherical Reflectance from a direction (Albedo): \[R_{\lambda}(\omega^{\text{in}}) = \iint_{\Omega^{\text{out}}} \rho_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) \cos\theta^{\text{out}} d\Omega^{\text{out}} \in [0, 1]\]

Albedo of diffuse surface, where \(\rho\) is constant for every direction: \[\alpha_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\pi} R_{\lambda}(\omega^{\text{in}})\]
