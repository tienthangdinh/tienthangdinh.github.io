The Rendering Equation

Basics

\[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\] whereby:

  • \(\cos\theta^{\text{in}}\): Lambertian cosine term. \(\theta^{\text{in}}\) is the angle between the incoming direction \(\omega^{\text{in}}\) and the surface normal \(\mathbf{n}\). The more senkrecht, the more energy reflected!

Official rendering equation: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) \mathbf{L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\text{trace}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}), -\omega^{\text{in}})} \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\]

And you know what is important? We have proved this: \[d\Omega^{\text{in}} = \sin\theta^{\text{in}} d\theta^{\text{in}} d\phi\]

Area-Based Rendering Equation: \[\Delta\Omega_x = \frac{\cos\theta^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}}{||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}||^2} \Delta A_y\] \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + \iint_{\mathbf{y} \in A} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\mathbf{y}, \omega^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}) \mathbf{V(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})} \frac{\cos\theta^{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{y}} \cos\theta^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}}{||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}||^2} d A_y\]

Full Geometry Term: \[G(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{V(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})}\frac{\cos\theta^{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{y}} \cos\theta^{\mathbf{y} \to \mathbf{x}}}{||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}||^2}\]

Recursive Operator Form of the Rendering Equation

\[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T} \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}\]

  • \(L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}\): The total outgoing radiance (the unknown we want to solve for).
  • \(L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}\): The emitted light (the light sources).
  • \(\mathbf{T}: L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) = L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}(\text{trace}(\mathbf{x}, \omega), -\omega)\)
  • \(\mathbf{R}: L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\)
  • \(\mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T}\): The combined light transport operator. It takes light that is already outgoing from a surface (\(L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}}\)), transports it to the next surface (\(T \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}\)), and reflects it (\(R \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}\)).

But how to solve this equation? \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T})^{-1} L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}}\] Using Taylor series expansion: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R} \mathbf{T} \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T})^2 \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T})^3 \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \dots\]

where \(\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T}\) means reflected once, \((\mathbf{R} \mathbf{T})^2\) means reflected twice, etc.

Approaches to solve rendering equation

Radiosity

Idea: All surfaces are diffuse => from Integral Equation (solved by Monte Carlo) to just a Linear Equation \(Ax=b\) solved by matrix inversion.

\[E_{\lambda, i}^{\text{out}} = E_{\lambda, i}^{\text{emit}} + \sum_{j} \rho_i F_{i \to j} E_{\lambda, j}^{\text{out}}\] where:

  • \(\rho_i\): The albedo of surface \(i\).
  • \(F_{i \to j}\): The form factor (geometric term) from surface \(i\) to surface \(j\).

Example

Imagine 4 patches forming a small square “room”:

  • Patch 1 (Top): A light source. \(E_{1}^{\text{emit}} = 100\), \(\rho_1 = 0\) (absorbing).
  • Patch 2 (Bottom): A reflective floor. \(E_{2}^{\text{emit}} = 0\), \(\rho_2 = 0.8\).
  • Patch 3 (Left): A wall. \(E_{3}^{\text{emit}} = 0\), \(\rho_3 = 0.5\).
  • Patch 4 (Right): A wall. \(E_{4}^{\text{emit}} = 0\), \(\rho_4 = 0.5\).

The Form Factors (\(F_{i \to j}\)) Assume the geometry is such that patches see each other with the following visibility:

  • Opposite patches (1 to 2, 3 to 4) have \(F = 0.6\).
  • Adjacent patches (1 to 3, 1 to 4, etc.) have \(F = 0.2\).
  • Patches cannot see themselves, so \(F_{ii} = 0\).

Step 1: Set up the Equations The radiosity equation for each patch is: \(E_i = E_i^{\text{emit}} + \rho_i \sum (F_{ij} E_j)\).

  • \(E_1\): \(100 + 0 = \mathbf{100}\) (Since \(\rho_1 = 0\), it only emits).
  • \(E_2\): \(0 + 0.8 \cdot (0.6 E_1 + 0.2 E_3 + 0.2 E_4)\)
  • \(E_3\): \(0 + 0.5 \cdot (0.2 E_1 + 0.2 E_2 + 0.6 E_4)\)
  • \(E_4\): \(0 + 0.5 \cdot (0.2 E_1 + 0.2 E_2 + 0.6 E_3)\)

Step 2: Solve Iteratively (Gathering) Because these equations are interdependent (e.g., \(E_3\) depends on \(E_4\), and \(E_4\) depends on \(E_3\)), we solve them in “bounces”.

  • Iteration 1 (Direct Light only)

We plug \(E_1 = 100\) into the others, assuming \(E_{2,3,4} = 0\) initially:

  • \(E_2\): \(0.8 \cdot (0.6 \cdot 100) = \mathbf{48.0}\)

  • \(E_3\): \(0.5 \cdot (0.2 \cdot 100) = \mathbf{10.0}\)

  • \(E_4\): \(0.5 \cdot (0.2 \cdot 100) = \mathbf{10.0}\)

  • Iteration 2 (First Reflection/Bounce)

Now we plug the values from Iteration 1 back into the equations to see how light bounces between the walls:

  • \(E_2\): \(0.8 \cdot (60 + 0.2 \cdot 10 + 0.2 \cdot 10) = 0.8 \cdot (64) = \mathbf{51.2}\)
  • \(E_3\): \(0.5 \cdot (20 + 0.2 \cdot 48 + 0.6 \cdot 10) = 0.5 \cdot (20 + 9.6 + 6) = \mathbf{17.8}\)
  • \(E_4\): \(0.5 \cdot (20 + 0.2 \cdot 48 + 0.6 \cdot 10) = 0.5 \cdot (20 + 9.6 + 6) = \mathbf{17.8}\)

Step 3: Final Results If you continue this for 4–5 iterations, the numbers will stop changing (converge).

  • Patch 1: 100 (The Sun)
  • Patch 2: \(\approx 54\) (Bright floor)
  • Patch 3 & 4: \(\approx 22\) (Dimmer walls)

Still this example, but what if not diffuse anymore?

Imagine Patch 2 is a mirror. Now, instead of one value for \(E_2\), you need an infinite number of values for every possible direction \(\omega\). So for every point, we have this directional-based integral again: \[L_o(x, \omega_o) = L_e(x, \omega_o) + \int_{\Omega} \rho(x, \omega_i, \omega_o) L_i(x, \omega_i) \cos \theta_i d\omega_i\]

So what change? For example:

  • Diffuse Rule: Light from the Bulb (1) hits the Floor (2) and splits 20% to Wall 3 and 20% to Wall 4.
  • Glossy Rule: Light from the Bulb (1) hits the Floor (2). Because of the angle, 90% of that reflected light is directed at Wall 4, and 0% goes to Wall 3.

So we dont have that 20% \(\rho\) for every direction anymore, we have \(\rho(x, \omega_i, \omega_o)\) always varying. We have to use Monte Carlo to sample the direction.

Path Tracing (Simple Version)

In Path Tracing, we start with \(N=1\) at the pixel, \(N=1\) at the first bounce, \(N=1\) at the second bounce, and so on, creates a single, complete light path. yeah basically \(N=1\) everywhere. So yes, only \(N=1\) sample per integration step.

The Approximation

When \(N=1\), the average \(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N\) becomes just a single term:\[F = \int f(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x} \approx \hat{f}_1 = \frac{f(\mathbf{x}_0)}{p(\mathbf{x}_0)} \quad \text{}\]

  • \(\mathbf{x}_0\): A single, randomly chosen light path direction
  • \(\hat{f}_1\): The result of this single, weighted sample

But if \(N=1\) then wehre the hell is the averaged estimation of Monte Carlo?

Averaging at the Pixel Level: THIS IS IMPORTANT!!! The averaging (\(\frac{1}{N}\)) is moved outside the recursion. To get the final image, the renderer traces millions of these \(N=1\) light paths (samples) for each pixel and averages the results together. This overall average still converges to the correct answer \(\bar{f}\), as proven by the Central Limit Theorem.

Bidirectional Path Tracing (BDPT)

Path Sampling both Direction is a Monte Carlo Framework

We want to build this path: \(y_0 \to \dots \to y_j \to x_i \to \dots \to x_1\). There are so many thing that we randomly sample:

  • Random point on light source
  • Random pixel
  • Random direction (BRDF)
  • Russian Roulette at each bounce

Each connection is a sample of the total light transport, so we Monte Carlo it: \[F \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{f(x_i)}{p(x_i)}\]

So what is Multi Importance Sampling (MIS) in BDPT?

Using your 4-patch example (Light, Floor, Walls), imagine a path with 3 segments: Light \(\to\) Floor \(\to\) Wall \(\to\) Eye. This path can be generated by:

  • Tracing 3 steps from the light and 0 from the eye.
  • Tracing 2 steps from the light and 1 from the eye.
  • Tracing 1 step from the light and 2 from the eye.

Each of these is a different sampling strategy for the exact same physical path. If you just added them together, the image would be far too bright and physically incorrect.

MIS assigns a weight (\(w\)) to each strategy so that the total contribution equals 1. It follows the Balance Heuristic: \[w_i(P) = \frac{p_i(P)}{\sum_{j} p_j(P)}\]

\(p_i(P)\): The probability of generating path \(P\) using strategy \(i\).

But how to assign those probabilities?

  1. Are probabilities for the Path or the Segment?

The probability \(p\) used in MIS is for the entire path. \[p(\text{Path}) = p(x_0) \cdot p(x_1|x_0) \cdot p(x_2|x_1) \dots\]

  1. How are \(p_A\) and \(p_B\) calculated automatically?

To calculate the PDF for a strategy, the computer looks at the sampling rules used during the Monte Carlo process.

  • Strategy A (Camera-heavy): \(p_A\) = (Pixel PDF) \(\times\) (BRDF PDF) \(\times\) (Geometry Factor).

  • Strategy B (Light-heavy): \(p_B\) = (Light Area PDF) \(\times\) (Emission PDF) \(\times\) (BRDF Reflection PDF).

Once the computer has these two numbers (\(p_A\) and \(p_B\)), it uses the Balance Heuristic automatically: \[w_B = \frac{p_B}{p_A + p_B}\]

If \(p_B\) is 1000 and \(p_A\) is 0.001 (because hitting a tiny bulb is hard), then \(w_B\) becomes 0.9999.The computer doesn’t “know” it’s a glossy floor; it just sees that the math for Strategy B resulted in a much higher probability density for that specific path than Strategy A did.

Backwards

Measurement Equation

Imagine exactly like a camera model. \(j\) is a pixel the image plane in the back. In front of the image plane is a hole/apeture of size \(A\), and \(x\) is a point on that hole/apeture.

The brightness (\(\Phi_{\lambda,j}\)) of a pixel \(j\): \[\Phi_{\lambda,j} = \iint_{x \in A} \iint_{\Omega_j^{in}(x)} W_\lambda^e(x, \omega^{in}) L_\lambda^{in}(x, \omega^{in}) \cos \theta^{in} d\Omega_j^{in} dA\]

  • It integrates all incoming radiance (\(L_\lambda^{in}\)) over each point \(x\) on the apeture \(A\), and each direction \(\omega^{in}\) that the light can come from. Some of the direction \(\omega^{in}\) going through point \(x\) will not point to pixel \(j\). But some will, we call this \(W_\lambda^e(x, \omega^{in})\) as follow. this part is the most confusing part for me until now.
  • If the ray coming from direction \(\omega^{\text{in}}\) at point \(\mathbf{x}\) hits the target pixel \(j\), then:\[W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = 1\]
  • If the ray misses pixel \(j\) or the entire sensor, then:\[W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = 0\]

Importance Function

Trace back a single race and see how much influence it has, 40% or 60%?… \[W_\lambda^{in}(x, \omega^{in}) = W_\lambda^e(x, \omega^{in}) + \iint_{\Omega^{out}} \rho(x, \omega^{out}, \omega^{in}) W_\lambda^{in}(trace(x, \omega^{out}), -\omega^{out}) \cos \theta^{out} d\Omega^{out}\]

How to use this? Let’s make a simplified example. We have two surfaces, A and B. We want to find the Incoming Importance (\(W^{\text{in}}\)) at a ray arriving at surface B from a light source.

  • Pixel (\(\mathbf{x}_0\)) has Sensor Efficiency \(W^e = W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = 1.0\) (Max influence)
  • Surface A (\(\mathbf{x}_1\)) has BRDF \(\rho_{A} = 0.5\) (Reflects 50% of influence)
  • Surface B (\(\mathbf{x}_2\)) has BRDF \(\rho_{B} = 0.8\) (Reflects 80% of influence)

Now we start at the sensor: \[W_{\lambda}^{in}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) = W^{\text{in}}_{\text{Pixel}} = W_{\lambda}^e(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}) + 0 = 1.0 + 0 = 1.0\]

That means influence leaving the pixel is \(W^{\text{out}}_{\text{Pixel}} = 1.0\).

Now we go to surface A. Surface A is the first surface hit by the ray traced backward from the pixel. Outgoing Importance \(W^{\text{out}}_{\text{A}}\) (Toward the Pixel): \[W^{\text{out}}_{\text{A} \to \text{Pixel}} = W^{\text{in}}_{\text{Pixel}} = 1.0\]

That means Influence leaving A toward the pixel = Influence arriving at the pixel from A.

Now we see what is the incoming importance at A- \(W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}}\): \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}} \approx \rho_{A} \cdot W^{\text{out}}_{\text{A} \to \text{Pixel}}\] \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}} = 0.5 \cdot 1.0 = 0.5\] That means a photon arriving at A only has \(50\%\) of the initial pixel’s influence, because A is a \(50\%\) reflector.

Surface B is hit after the ray scatters from A. Influence leaving B toward A = Influence arriving at A from B. \[W^{\text{out}}_{\text{B} \to \text{A}} = W^{\text{in}}_{\text{A}} = 0.5\]

Now Incoming Importance (\(W^{\text{in}}\)) at B. (which flows back to the light source): \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{B}} \approx \rho_{B} \cdot W^{\text{out}}_{\text{B} \to \text{A}}\] \[W^{\text{in}}_{\text{B}} = 0.8 \cdot 0.5 = 0.4\]

Conclusion: The influence of \(1.0\) started at the pixel, traveled backward, lost \(50\%\) at surface A, and lost \(20\%\) at surface B, resulting in an overall accumulated influence of \(0.4\) for the entire path. The renderer now knows that if it finds a light path ending at B, that light must be weighted by \(0.4\) to contribute correctly to the pixel.

Radiometry

Let’s just start this simple, each chronomotic light in a interval of time \(t\) brings some kind of power: \[\Phi_{\lambda,t} = \frac{dQ{\lambda}}{dt} \quad (W)\]

Irradiance is how much of that power in an area from ALL DIRECTIONS: \[H_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{d\Phi_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}}{dA} \quad (W/m^2)\]

Radiance is how much of that power IN 1 SPECIFIC DIRECTION, REMEMBER ONLY DIRECTION, AREA DOESNT MATTER: \[L_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) = \frac{d^2\Phi_{\lambda}}{dA_{\perp} d\Omega} \quad (\text{W} \cdot \text{m}^{-2} \cdot \text{sr}^{-1})\]

Since the area in radiance does not matter, if we want to convert into irradiance, \(dA = \frac{dA_{\perp}}{\cos\theta}\).

So we could see direct relationship between irradiance and radiance: \[H_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}) = \iint_{\Omega} L_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) \cos\theta d\Omega\]

BRDF: \[\rho_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) = \frac{dL_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}})}{dH_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}})}\]

whereby: \(dH_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\)

Hemispherical Reflectance from a direction (Albedo): \[R_{\lambda}(\omega^{\text{in}}) = \iint_{\Omega^{\text{out}}} \rho_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) \cos\theta^{\text{out}} d\Omega^{\text{out}} \in [0, 1]\]

Albedo of diffuse surface, where \(\rho\) is constant for every direction: \[\alpha_{\lambda}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\pi} R_{\lambda}(\omega^{\text{in}})\]

Monte Carlo Integration

Why do we even need this thing?

We all know that the rendering equation has 2 infinities: infinite dimensions and infinite samples: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{out}} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{T})^{-1} L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R}\mathbf{T} \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R}\mathbf{T})^2 \cdot L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit}} + \dots \quad \text{}\] This is a \(2 \times 2 + 2 \times 2 + 2 \times 2 + \dots = \infty\) dimensional integral. It is not like the same BRDF, here we zweig the directions too. So we still sum up, but using Monte Carlo philosophy, using importance sampling to approximate the integral.

VERY IMPORTANT ALERT

For normal integration, error grows with the dimensional-root of the number of samples (\(\epsilon = \Delta f / \sqrt[d]{N}\)). Remember the triangles? yes! => But Monte Carlo error only grows with the square root of the number of samples (\(\sigma[\hat{f}_N] = \sigma_f / \sqrt{N}\)), regardless of the dimension of the integral.

Formulate Monte Carlo Integration

The ultimate goal is to estimate an integral or expectation value. \[F = \int f(x) dx\] But well, we could not afford to get all samples. So the magic happens when you realize that the integral \(F\) can be estimated by simply taking the average of just a small amount of samples \(N\).

BUT with a condition, weighted each sample by its own probability density function \(p(\mathbf{x})\). \[\hat{F} = \frac{1}{N} \sum \frac{f(x_i)}{p(x_i)}\] Actually, it is: \[\hat{F} = E [\frac{1}{N} \sum \frac{f(x_i)}{p(x_i)}]\]

Because when we calculation multiple times, each time we get different result, and this means we take average of all of these results.

The more samples we have, the smaller the variance \(V[\hat{F}]\) and its standard deviation \(\sigma[\hat{F}]\): \[V[\hat{F}] = \frac{\sigma^2}{N} \quad \text{and} \quad \sigma[\hat{F}] = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\]

Where \(\sigma\) is the real standard deviation of the function \(f(x)\).

And you see what? This solves the curse of dimensionality! That means, the statistical error decreases with the square root of the number of samples (\(O(1/\sqrt{N})\)), regardless of the dimension of the integral.

Importance Sampling

OK, now we have created the framework - Monte Carlo. But well, we could not afford to get many samples, we need to be a little strategic about what samples to take so that the variance is minimized.

So the ideal case is, the sampling distribution \(p(\mathbf{x})\) is chosen to be proportional to the function \(f(\mathbf{x})\). \[p(\mathbf{x}) \propto f(\mathbf{x})\]

Since \(p(\mathbf{x})\) must be a valid probability density function, the integral over the entire domain \(\Omega\) must be \(1\) (\(\int p(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x} = 1\)). This requires \(p(\mathbf{x})\) to be normalized by the true value of the integral (\(F = \int f(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x}\)): \[p(\mathbf{x}) = c \cdot f(\mathbf{x}), \quad \text{where } c = \frac{1}{F}\] \[p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{f(\mathbf{x})}{F} = \frac{f(\mathbf{x})}{\int f(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x}}\]

THIS IS SUPER IMPORTANT, WITHOUT THIS F, THERE WOULD BE NO SUM OF LIGHTS, OTHERWISE WOULD BE ONLY AVERAGED LIGHT

Example

Step 1: Taking F from BRDF

Let’s say at a bounce the BRDF of incoming light intensity \(f(x)\) from angles between 0 and 2. We guess that the actual light intensity is also similar. \[f(x) = 2x\] First, we must calculate \(F\), basically integrating the BRDF. \[F = \int_{0}^{2} f(x) \, dx = \int_{0}^{2} 2x \, dx\] \[F = [x^2]_0^2 = 2^2 - 0^2 = \mathbf{4}\] BRDF is 4. If our Monte Carlo algorithm is good and the light really has the same intensity as BRDF, it will give us 4.

Step 2: Importance Sampling PDF from BRDF

Now, we are going to build our Probability Density Function \(p(x)\) using your exact formula: \(p(x) = \frac{f(x)}{F}\).Since we know \(f(x) = 2x\) and \(F = 4\), we plug them in: \[p(x) = \frac{2x}{4} = \frac{x}{2}\]

Step 3: The Monte Carlo Estimate

What else, just plug numbers in: \[\hat{F} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{f(x_i)}{p(x_i)}\]

Let’s take just one single random sample (\(N=1\)).

Scenario A: The computer randomly picks \(x = 1.5\) \[\hat{F} = \frac{f(1.5)}{p(1.5)} = \frac{3}{0.75} = \mathbf{4}\]

Result: 4. With just ONE single ray, we got the exact perfect answer.

Scenario B: The computer randomly picks a different number, \(x = 0.5\) \[\hat{F} = \frac{f(0.5)}{p(0.5)} = \frac{1}{0.25} = \mathbf{4}\]

Result: 4.

In reality, we actually dont know \(F\), so we “guess” a \(p(x)\) that looks similar to \(f(x)\) (mostly using the BRDF lobe), but it won’t be perfectly proportional. Because it’s not perfect, the math won’t cancel out cleanly to 4 every single time. Sometimes you’ll get 3.8, sometimes 4.2. Therefore we do it many times and take the average.

The stupid Ray Tracing family

Classic Ray Tracing (everything is deterministic)

For every pixel, you fire one ray. When it hits a surface, it does three things:

  • Check if a light is visible (Shadow Ray).
  • Bounce in the exact mirror direction (Reflection Ray).
  • Bend in the exact refraction direction (Refraction Ray).

Result: Everything looks perfectly sharp. Shadows have hard edges, mirrors are perfect, and glass has no blur.

Weakness: It cannot simulate the “fuzziness” of the real world (like soft shadows or glossy surfaces). Therefore the upcoming Distributed Ray Tracing was introduced

Distributed Ray Tracing (probabilistic model with Monte Carlo)

It does some things differently:

  • Many rays per pixel
  • Many reflection samples per bounce, not just one mirror reflection => fuzzy blurry effect

Achieved Goal: blurry effect

Weakness: After each bounce, the number of rays grow exponentially.

Path Tracing (basically Distributed Ray Tracing with \(N=1\)) (also probabilistic model with Monte Carlo)

At each bounce, one direct and one indirect ray is sampled. remember, AT EACH BOUNCE !!! \[L_\lambda^{\text{out}} = L_\lambda^{\text{emit}} + \mathbf{R}\mathbf{T} \cdot L_\lambda^{\text{emit}} + (\mathbf{R}\mathbf{T})^2 \cdot L_\lambda^{\text{emit}} + \dots\]

Using Monte Carlo estimation: \[L_\lambda^{\text{out}}(\underline{x}_0, \omega_0^1) = \frac{w_{\lambda,1}}{p_1} \cdot \left( \frac{w_{\lambda,2}}{p_2} \cdot \left( \dots \left( \frac{w_{\lambda,n}}{p_n} L_{\lambda,n+1}^e \right) \dots \right) \right)\]

where \[w_{\lambda,j} = \rho(\dots) \cos\theta\] is the “weight” of the light of that direction

And you know what, it absolutely looks like this: \[L_\lambda^{\text{out}} = \frac{w_1 \cdot w_2 \cdot \dots \cdot w_n}{p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \dots \cdot p_n} \cdot L^{\text{emit}}\] \[\hat{J} = \frac{f_0 \cdot f_1 \cdot \dots \cdot f_n}{p_0 \cdot p_1 \cdot \dots \cdot p_n}\]

Useless effects

Depth of Field

So we want to model the blurriness of the lens. pixels are only sharp when they are placed at focal plane, if they are placed a little front or back, it becomes blur. And for a pixel, we integrate over all point x on the pixel area on this focal plane, and over allapeture area. \[\Phi_{\lambda, j} = \mathbf{M}_{\lambda, j}^{\Omega^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}) \times A_j} L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}} \quad \text{}\] * \(\Phi_{\lambda, j}\) total spectral power (flux) for pixel \(j\) (brightness) * \(\mathbf{M}_{\lambda, j}^{\Omega^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x}) \times A_j}\) (The Measurement Operator) * \(A_j\): The integral is performed over the pixel area defined on the focal plane. * \(\Omega^{\text{in}}(\mathbf{x})\): The integral is performed over the solid angle of directions that pass through the lens aperture (\(a\)) and contribute to the point \(\mathbf{x}\) on the focal plane. * \(L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}\) is just incoming light solved by the Rendering Equation

Spatial Filtering

Goal: When a ray tracer calculates the color of a pixel, it draws multiple samples (light paths) within the pixel area. If these samples are simply averaged without weighting, it corresponds to a box filter. However, it can create Aliasing idk why.

To fix, it is really simple! Weight the samples using a Gaußian filter. This filter weights samples closer to the pixel center more heavily than samples near the edges. \[\text{Pixel Color} = \int_{-1}^{1} L(x) \cdot k(x) \, dx\]

Temporal Filtering

Goal: when the camera’s shutter is open for a finite amount of time, causing moving objects to appear stretched.

This is done in the same way as spatial filtering, but instead of integrating over the pixel area (\(x, y\)), the renderer integrates over a time interval. For example, a pixel might see different part of a moving ball at different timestamp when it emits the exact same ray direction!

Russian Roulette

At each bounce, a random decision is made to either continue tracing the path or terminate it.

Monte Carlo-based Ray Tracing (All about Scaling Up!!!)

General idea: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\omega^{\text{out}}) = \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(\omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\omega^{\text{in}})\cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}} \quad \text{}\] is estimated by Monte Carlo with importance sampling but without MIS: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\omega^{\text{out}}) \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{\rho(\omega_i^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}})L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\omega_i^{\text{in}})\cos\theta_i^{\text{in}}}{p(\omega_i^{\text{in}})} \quad \text{}\]

where:

  • \(\frac{1}{p(\omega_i^{\text{in}})}\) replaces the \(\iint d\Omega^{\text{in}}\) integration.
  • \(f(\omega^{\text{in}}) = \rho(\omega_i^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}})L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\omega_i^{\text{in}})\cos\theta_i^{\text{in}}\), is the function that the PDF \(p(\omega^{\text{in}})\) should ideally be proportional to. We cannot sample all 3 components at once, so the PDF of each component must be split up.
    • Cosine-weighted sampling using Nusselt’s analogy
    • BRDF sampling by splitting again \(\rho = \rho_{diff} + \rho_{spec} + \rho_{mirr}\) (for e.g. use 80% diffuse, 20% specular)
    • Direct light sampling I still dont know what to sample here when we already know the direction.

To put all these strategies together, at each bounce, we calculate the contribution of direct light (Area light sampling) and indirect light (cosine+BRDF) separately and take average of their weighted sum by MIS: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}} = L_{\lambda}^{\text{direct}} (\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) + L_{\lambda}^{\text{indirect}} (\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) \quad \text{}\]

Example 1 only One Strategy (no MIS)

Once again, Monte Carlo with importance sampling but without MIS: \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{reflect}}(\omega^{\text{out}}) \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{\rho(\omega_i^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}})L_{\lambda}^{\text{in}}(\omega_i^{\text{in}})\cos\theta_i^{\text{in}}}{p(\omega_i^{\text{in}})} \quad \text{}\]

We are going to shoot \(N = 2\) random rays out from a diffuse floor. BRDF is \(\rho = \frac{0.5}{\pi}\) (Albedo of diffuse material always divide by \(\pi\) to conserve energy). \[p(\omega_i^{\text{in}}) = \frac{\cos\theta_i^{\text{in}}}{\pi}\] we sample the light based on Nusselt’s analogy.

Ray 1 hits \(L^{\text{in}}_1\) with 100 unit of light, and angle \(\cos\theta_1\) of 60 degrees. \[\text{Value}_1 = \frac{\rho \cdot L^{\text{in}}_1 \cdot \cos\theta_1}{p(\omega_1)} = \frac{\frac{0.5}{\pi} \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1}{2}}{\frac{\cos\theta_1}{\pi}} = 50\]

Ray 2 hits \(L^{\text{in}}_2\) dark ceiling with 10 unit of light, and angle \(\cos\theta_2\) of 0 degrees. \[\text{Value}_2 = \frac{\rho \cdot L^{\text{in}}_2 \cdot \cos\theta_2}{p(\omega_2)} = \frac{\frac{0.5}{\pi} \cdot 10 \cdot 0}{\frac{\cos\theta_2}{\pi}} = 5\]

Average: \[\text{Average} = \frac{\text{Value}_1 + \text{Value}_2}{2} = \frac{50 + 5}{2} = 27.5\]

Example 2 with more Strategies (with MIS)

Now we use MIS to combine two strategies (2 rays) at the exact same time: Direct Light Sampling and BRDF Sampling. We add an extra weight before each light. THIS EXTRA WEIGHT IS ALL ABOUT THE TRUST TO THAT METHOD \[\hat{f}^N_{\text{MIS}} = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{n_i} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} \omega_i(x_{i,j}) \frac{f(x_{i,j})}{p_i(x_{i,j})} \quad \text{}\] \[\hat{f}^N_{\text{MIS}} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{n_1} \sum_{j=1}^{n_1} \omega_1(x_{1,j}) \frac{f(x_{1,j})}{p_1(x_{1,j})}}_{\text{Contribution from Strategy 1 (e.g., Light Sampling)}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{n_2} \sum_{j=1}^{n_2} \omega_2(x_{2,j}) \frac{f(x_{2,j})}{p_2(x_{2,j})}}_{\text{Contribution from Strategy 2 (e.g., BRDF Sampling)}} + \dots\]

For Path Tracing: \[\text{Final Color} = \left[ w_{\text{light}} \cdot \frac{f(x_1)}{p_{\text{light}}(x_1)} \right] + \left[ w_{\text{brdf}} \cdot \frac{f(x_2)}{p_{\text{brdf}}(x_2)} \right]\] where \(f = \rho \cdot L^{\text{in}} \cdot \cos\theta\)

Ray 1: Direct Light Sampling

A random point on a light bulb is picked, the light intensity \(f = \mathbf{90}\) and:

  • \(p_{\text{light}}\): The probability that the Light Sampler picked this spot is 0.4 (because aiming at the light is its only job).
  • \(p_{\text{brdf}}\): The probability that the BRDF Sampler would have randomly fired in this dull direction is very low, just 0.05.

\[L_{\text{direct}} = \frac{p_{\text{light}}}{p_{\text{light}} + p_{\text{brdf}}} \cdot \frac{f(x_1)}{p_{\text{light}}(x_1)} = \frac{0.4}{0.4 + 0.05} \cdot \frac{90}{0.4} = \frac{0.4}{0.45} \cdot 225 = 0.888 * 225 = \mathbf{200}\]

Ray 2: BRDF Sampling

Second ray follows BRDF lobe and accidentally got \(f = \mathbf{400}\):

  • \(p_{\text{light}}\): The probability that the Light Sampler would have picked this exact sub-pixel coordinate on the lamp is lower, say 0.14.
  • \(p_{\text{brdf}}\): The probability that the BRDF Sampler fired here is 0.35 (because it heavily favors shiny angles).

\[L_{\text{brdf}} = \frac{p_{\text{brdf}}}{p_{\text{light}} + p_{\text{brdf}}} \cdot \frac{f(x_2)}{p_{\text{brdf}}(x_2)} = \frac{0.35}{0.14 + 0.35} \cdot \frac{400}{0.35} = \frac{0.35}{0.49} \cdot \frac{400}{0.35} = \frac{400}{0.49} \approx \mathbf{816}\]

To get the final color, we just add the weighted contributions from our two strategies together: \[\text{Final Color} = L_{\text{direct}} + L_{\text{brdf}} = 200 + 816 = \mathbf{1016}\]

Indirect light sampling (keyword: cosin & BRDF)

To calculate indirect light: \[L_\lambda^{\text{indirect}}(x, \omega^{\text{out}}) = \iint_{\Omega^{\text{in}}} \rho(x, \omega^{\text{in}}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_\lambda^{\text{reflect}}(\text{trace}(x, \omega^{\text{in}}), -\omega^{\text{in}}) \cos\theta^{\text{in}} d\Omega^{\text{in}}\]

1. BRDF sampling

I think we all know what that is, quite intuitive that we want to sample according to BRDF lobe. But let’s discuss about this in another post.

2. Cosine sampling

Area of a unit circle is \(\pi r^2 = \pi (1)^2 = \pi\), so PDF is \(p(x, y) = 1/\pi\). We use this as the base to calculate PDF of each direction \(p_{\text{Nusselt}}(\omega^{in}) = \cos\theta^{in}/\pi\).

Imagine we sample N random direction, this is our Monte Carlo estimation: \[L_{\lambda,\text{Nusselt}}^{\text{reflect}}(\omega^{out}) \approx\frac{1}{N} \sum \frac{\rho(\omega_i^{in}, \omega^{out}) \cdot L_\lambda^{in}(\omega_i^{in}) \cdot \cos\theta^{in}}{\left(\frac{\cos\theta^{in}}{\pi}\right)}\] \[L_{\lambda,\text{Nusselt}}^{\text{reflect}}(\omega^{out}) \approx \frac{\pi}{N} \sum_i \rho(\omega_i^{in}, \omega^{out}) L_\lambda^{in}(\omega_i^{in})\]

Direct light sampling (keyword: Area)

FOr direct light sampling we rewrite into integral over areas: \[L_{\lambda,l}^{\text{direct,area}}(x, \omega^{\text{out}}) = \iint_{y \in A_l} \rho \cdot L_{\lambda,l}^{\text{emit,area}} \cdot V(x, y) \frac{\cos\theta^{x \to y} \cos\theta^{y \to x}}{\|x - y\|^2} dA_y\]

We can also estimate this using Monte Carlo:

  • For each area light \(l\), we need to sample a number \(N_l\) of points \(\mathbf{y}_i\) on the light source.
  • The integral is then approximated by a sum, where each sample \(\mathbf{y}_i\) is weighted by \(1/p(\mathbf{y}_i)\).
  • \(p(\mathbf{y}_i)\): The Probability Density Function for sampling a point \(\mathbf{y}_i\) on the area light. \[L_{\lambda}^{\text{direct,area}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\text{out}}) \approx \frac{1}{N_l} \sum_{i=1}^{N_l} \frac{\rho(\mathbf{x}, \omega^{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{y}_i}, \omega^{\text{out}}) L_{\lambda}^{\text{emit,area}}(\mathbf{y}_i, \omega^{\mathbf{y}_i \to \mathbf{x}}) G(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}_i)}{p(\mathbf{y}_i)} \quad \text{}\]
